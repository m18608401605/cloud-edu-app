Consumers, Consumption and Choice

In the previous weeks, we discussed markets, demand and supply and equilibrium. Now we will focus on the demand side: consumers.

Consumption refers to the act of using goods and services to satisfy wants and needs. It is a fundamental component of economic activity and plays a crucial role in determining demand in markets. When consumers make choices about what to buy and consume, they are influenced by various factors such as income, prices, preferences, and the utility (satisfaction) they expect to derive from different goods and services.

Key aspects of consumption:

  • Utility: The satisfaction or benefit that consumers derive from consuming goods and services. It is a subjective measure of happiness or well-being.
  • Preferences: The tastes and desires of consumers, which determine how they rank different bundles of goods and services.
  • Budget Constraints: The limited income of consumers restricts what they can purchase. They must make choices within their budget.
  • Rational Choice: Consumers are assumed to be rational, meaning they aim to maximize their utility given their budget constraint.
  • Substitution and Income Effects: Changes in prices affect consumption decisions through these two effects.
  • Diminishing Marginal Utility: The principle that as a consumer consumes more of a good, the additional satisfaction (marginal utility) from each additional unit tends to decrease.

Utility Theory

Utility is a measure of the satisfaction or happiness that a consumer derives from consuming a good or service. It is a key concept in microeconomics used to understand consumer behavior and choice.

Key Concepts:

Total Utility (TU) - The total satisfaction a consumer gets from consuming a certain quantity of a good or service. It is the sum of the utility derived from each unit consumed.
Marginal Utility (MU) - The additional utility a consumer gets from consuming one more unit of a good or service. It measures the change in total utility resulting from a one-unit increase in consumption.
MU = ΔTU / ΔQ

where ΔTU is the change in total utility and ΔQ is the change in quantity consumed.

Law of Diminishing Marginal Utility - As a consumer consumes more units of a good, the marginal utility derived from each additional unit decreases, while total utility continues to increase at a decreasing rate.

Consumer Equilibrium

Consumer Equilibrium occurs when a consumer allocates their income in such a way that they maximize their total utility given their budget constraint.

Condition for Consumer Equilibrium:
MUX / PX = MUY / PY = ... = MUn / Pn = λ

where:

  • MUX, MUY, ... = Marginal utility of goods X, Y, etc.
  • PX, PY, ... = Price of goods X, Y, etc.
  • λ = Marginal utility of money (constant)

This condition states that at equilibrium, the marginal utility per dollar spent is equal across all goods. If MUX/PX > MUY/PY, the consumer should buy more X and less Y to increase total utility.

Example: Suppose a consumer has $100 to spend on two goods: pizza (P = $10) and burgers (P = $5). The marginal utilities are:

Pizza: MU1 = 50, MU2 = 40, MU3 = 30
Burgers: MU1 = 30, MU2 = 25, MU3 = 20

For 2 pizzas and 4 burgers:
MUpizza / Ppizza = 40 / 10 = 4
MUburger / Pburger = 20 / 5 = 4
Since they are equal, consumer equilibrium is achieved.

Indifference Curve Analysis

Indifference Curve - A curve that shows all combinations of two goods that provide the consumer with the same level of utility or satisfaction.

The consumer is indifferent between any two points on the same indifference curve, as they provide the same level of satisfaction.

Properties of Indifference Curves:

  1. Convex to the origin: Reflects diminishing marginal rate of substitution. The indifference curve bows inward toward the origin.
  2. Higher curves represent higher utility: Points on higher indifference curves represent bundles with more utility. U3 > U2 > U1.
  3. Indifference curves do not intersect: Each bundle belongs to only one indifference curve. Intersection would violate transitivity.
  4. Negatively sloped: To maintain the same utility level, if more of one good is consumed, less of the other must be consumed.

Marginal Rate of Substitution (MRS)

Marginal Rate of Substitution (MRSXY) - The rate at which a consumer is willing to trade one good for another while remaining on the same indifference curve.
MRSXY = -ΔY / ΔX = MUX / MUY

where:

  • MRSXY is the marginal rate of substitution of X for Y
  • -ΔY/ΔX is the absolute value of the slope of the indifference curve
  • MUX/MUY is the ratio of marginal utilities

The MRS is negative because to keep utility constant, an increase in X must be accompanied by a decrease in Y (or vice versa). By convention, we often refer to the absolute value of MRS.

Diminishing MRS: As a consumer consumes more of good X and less of good Y, the marginal rate of substitution of X for Y decreases. This is because the consumer values X less (due to diminishing MU) and values Y more.
Example: On an indifference curve:
At point A: To get 1 more unit of X, the consumer is willing to give up 5 units of Y. MRS = 5.
At point B: To get 1 more unit of X, the consumer is willing to give up only 2 units of Y. MRS = 2.
Thus, MRS diminishes as we move down the indifference curve (more X, less Y).

Budget Line

Budget Line - A line that shows all combinations of two goods that a consumer can purchase given their income and the prices of the goods.
I = PX · X + PY · Y

where:

  • I = Consumer's income
  • PX, PY = Prices of goods X and Y
  • X, Y = Quantities of goods X and Y

Budget Line Characteristics:

  • Y-intercept: I/PY - Maximum quantity of Y when X = 0
  • X-intercept: I/PX - Maximum quantity of X when Y = 0
  • Slope: -PX/PY - Opportunity cost of X in terms of Y
Slope = -PX / PY
Y I/PX ΔX = I/PX ΔY = I/PY

Consumer Optimum

Consumer Optimum occurs at the point where the highest attainable indifference curve is tangent to the budget line. At this point, the consumer maximizes utility subject to the budget constraint.

First Order Condition (Tangency Condition):
MRSXY = PX / PY

or equivalently:

MUX / PX = MUY / PY

Interpretation:

  • MRSXY = rate at which consumer is willing to trade Y for X
  • PX/PY = rate at which the market allows consumer to trade Y for X
  • At optimum: willingness to trade = market's rate of trade

Effects of Changes in Income and Prices

1. Income Effect

When income changes, the budget line shifts parallel to itself:

  • Increase in income: Budget line shifts outward (consumer can afford more)
  • Decrease in income: Budget line shifts inward (consumer can afford less)

The slope of the budget line remains unchanged because prices (and therefore the price ratio) are constant.

1 (lower income) I2 (higher income) I0 (original) Increase in income
Figure 5: Income Effect on Budget Line

2. Price Effect

When the price of one good changes, the budget line rotates:

  • Decrease in PX: Budget line rotates outward around the Y-intercept (consumer can buy more X)
  • Increase in PX: Budget line rotates inward around the Y-intercept (consumer can buy less X)
  • Decrease in PY: Budget line rotates outward around the X-intercept
  • Increase in PY: Budget line rotates inward around the X-intercept

The Y-intercept (I/PY) remains unchanged when only PX changes, and the X-intercept (I/PX) remains unchanged when only PY changes.

Decomposition of Price Effect

The price effect can be decomposed into two effects:

Price Effect = Substitution Effect + Income Effect

Substitution Effect (SE)

The change in consumption due to the change in relative prices, keeping utility constant. The consumer substitutes toward the relatively cheaper good.

  • Always moves in the opposite direction of the price change
  • If PX falls: SE increases consumption of X
  • If PX rises: SE decreases consumption of X

Income Effect (IE)

The change in consumption due to the change in purchasing power. A price decrease effectively increases real income.

  • Direction depends on whether the good is normal or inferior
  • Normal good: IE reinforces SE (buy more when real income increases)
  • Inferior good: IE opposes SE (buy less when real income increases)

Normal vs. Inferior Goods

Normal Goods

Normal Good - A good for which demand increases when income increases (and decreases when income decreases).

For normal goods:

  • Income elasticity of demand is positive (EI > 0)
  • Income effect reinforces the substitution effect
  • When PX falls: SE increases X, IE increases X → Total effect = increase in X

Inferior Goods

Inferior Good - A good for which demand decreases when income increases (and increases when income decreases).

For inferior goods:

  • Income elasticity of demand is negative (EI < 0)
  • Income effect works against the substitution effect
  • When PX falls: SE increases X, IE decreases X → Total effect depends on relative magnitudes
Examples:
Normal goods: Restaurant meals, new cars, electronics
Inferior goods: Instant noodles, used clothing, public transportation
Page 12

Giffen Goods (Special Case)

Giffen Good - A special type of inferior good where the negative income effect is so strong that it outweighs the substitution effect, resulting in a positive relationship between price and quantity demanded (violating the law of demand).

For Giffen goods:

  • When PX rises: SE decreases X (negative), IE increases X (positive), and |IE| > |SE|
    → Net effect: Quantity demanded increases as price rises
  • When PX falls: SE increases X (positive), IE decreases X (negative), and |IE| > |SE|
    → Net effect: Quantity demanded decreases as price falls
  • This creates an upward-sloping demand curve
Note: Giffen goods are primarily theoretical. They require:
  • The good must be inferior
  • The good must take a large portion of the consumer's budget
  • There must be no close substitutes
The classic example is the Irish potato famine: as potato prices rose, poor people could afford less meat and had to buy more potatoes to survive.

Price Consumption Curve (PCC) - The locus of optimal consumption bundles as the price of one good varies, holding income and the price of the other good constant.

The PCC shows how the optimal bundle changes as we rotate the budget line by changing the price of one good.

Engel Curve

Engel Curve - A curve showing the relationship between the quantity demanded of a good and the consumer's income, holding prices constant.

The Engel curve is derived from the Income Consumption Curve (ICC). It plots the quantity of a good on the horizontal axis against income on the vertical axis.

Types of Engel Curves

  1. Normal Good (Positive Slope): Quantity increases with income
  2. Inferior Good (Negative Slope): Quantity decreases with income

Classification of Normal Goods by Income Elasticity:

Necessity 0 < EI < 1 Upward sloping, flattening Quantity increases less than proportionally
Luxury EI > 1 Upward sloping, steepening Quantity increases more than proportionally

Special Cases of Preferences

1. Perfect Substitutes

Perfect Substitutes - Goods that can replace each other at a constant rate. The consumer is always willing to substitute one good for another at a fixed ratio.

Examples: Butter and margarine, Pepsi and Coke, different brands of bottled water

Utility: U(X, Y) = aX + bY
MRSXY = a/b (constant)

Characteristics:

  • Indifference curves are straight, parallel lines
  • MRS is constant along each indifference curve
  • Corner solution: Consumer buys only the good with lower marginal utility per dollar
1 U2 U3 Corner solution
Figure 12: Perfect Substitutes
Page 17

2. Perfect Complements

Perfect Complements - Goods that are consumed together in fixed proportions. The consumer gains no additional utility from having more of one good without having a proportional increase in the other.

Examples: Left and right shoes, cars and tires, coffee and sugar (for some consumers)

Utility: U(X, Y) = min{aX, bY}

Characteristics:

  • Indifference curves are L-shaped
  • The corner of each L is at the fixed ratio a:b
  • MRS is undefined at the corner and 0 or ∞ elsewhere
  • Corner solution: Consumer always consumes at the kink (fixed ratio)
Figure 13: Perfect Complements

3. Cobb-Douglas Preferences

Cobb-Douglas Utility Function - A widely used form of utility function with well-behaved properties (convex, smooth indifference curves).
U(X, Y) = Xα Yβ

where α, β > 0. Often we normalize so that α + β = 1.

Characteristics:

  • Indifference curves are convex to the origin
  • MRS diminishes as X increases
  • Both goods are always consumed in positive amounts (interior solution)

Marginal Utilities:

MUX = αXα-1Yβ = α(Y/X)
MUY = βXαYβ-1 = β(X/Y)

MRS:

MRSXY = MUX / MUY = (α/β)(Y/X)

Demand Functions for Cobb-Douglas:

X = (α/(α + β)) × (I/PX)
Y = (β/(α + β)) × (I/PY)
Key Property: For Cobb-Douglas utility, the consumer spends a constant share of income on each good. Share on X = α/(α + β), share on Y = β/(α + β).

Lagrangian Method for Constrained Optimization

The consumer's problem is to maximize utility subject to the budget constraint. This constrained optimization problem can be solved using the Lagrangian method.

The Consumer's Problem:
Max U(X, Y) subject to I = PXX + PYY

Setting up the Lagrangian:

ℒ(X, Y, λ) = U(X, Y) + λ(I - PXX - PYY)

where λ (lambda) is the Lagrange multiplier, representing the marginal utility of income.

First-Order Conditions (FOCs):

∂ℒ/∂X = MUX - λPX = 0
∂ℒ/∂Y = MUY - λPY = 0
∂ℒ/∂λ = I - PXX - PYY = 0

From the first two FOCs:

MUX/PX = MUY/PY = λ
Page 20

Interpretation of the Lagrange Multiplier (λ)

The Lagrange multiplier λ represents the marginal utility of income. It tells us how much utility would increase if income increased by one unit.

λ = ∂U*/∂I

where U* is the maximum utility at the optimal bundle.

Example: If λ = 2 at the optimum, then increasing income by $1 would increase maximum utility by approximately 2 utils.

Example: Solving with the Lagrangian

Maximize U(X, Y) = X0.5Y0.5 subject to 100 = 2X + 5Y

Step 1: Set up the Lagrangian

ℒ = X0.5Y0.5 + λ(100 - 2X - 5Y)

Step 2: Find partial derivatives and set to zero

∂ℒ/∂X = 0.5X-0.5Y0.5 - 2λ = 0
∂ℒ/∂Y = 0.5X0.5Y-0.5 - 5λ = 0

Step 3: Solve for λ and eliminate

From ∂ℒ/∂X: λ = 0.25X-0.5Y0.5

From ∂ℒ/∂Y: λ = 0.1X0.5Y-0.5

Setting equal: 0.25X-0.5Y0.5 = 0.1X0.5Y-0.5

2.5Y/X = X/Y → 2.5Y2 = X2 → X = √2.5 Y ≈ 1.58Y

Page 21

Step 4: Substitute into the budget constraint

100 = 2(1.58Y) + 5Y = 3.16Y + 5Y = 8.16Y

Y = 100/8.16 ≈ 12.25

X = 1.58 × 12.25 ≈ 19.35

Step 5: Verify

Budget: 2(19.35) + 5(12.25) = 38.7 + 61.25 = 99.95 ≈ 100 ✓

Practice Problems

Problem 1: A consumer's utility function is U(X, Y) = 3X + 2Y. The prices are PX = 5 and PY = 3, and income I = 60.

a) What type of utility function is this?
b) What is the consumer's optimal bundle?
Solution:
a) This is a perfect substitutes utility function (linear form).
b) Compare MU/P ratios:
MUX = 3, MUY = 2
MUX/PX = 3/5 = 0.6
MUY/PY = 2/3 = 0.67
Since MUY/PY > MUX/PX, the consumer buys only Y.
Optimal: X = 0, Y = 60/3 = 20
Problem 2: A consumer's utility function is U(X, Y) = min{2X, 4Y}. The prices are PX = 2 and PY = 3, and income I = 40.

a) What type of utility function is this?
b) What is the consumer's optimal bundle?
Solution:
a) This is a perfect complements utility function (Leontief form).
b) For optimal consumption: 2X = 4Y → X = 2Y
Budget: 40 = 2X + 3Y = 2(2Y) + 3Y = 7Y
Y = 40/7 ≈ 5.71, X = 2(5.71) ≈ 11.43

Problem 3: A consumer's utility function is U(X, Y) = X0.3Y0.7. Income is I = 200, PX = 4, PY = 5.

a) Find the optimal quantities of X and Y.
b) What share of income is spent on each good?
c) Calculate the maximum utility at the optimum.
Solution:
a) For Cobb-Douglas with α = 0.3, β = 0.7:
X = (0.3/1) × (200/4) = 0.3 × 50 = 15
Y = (0.7/1) × (200/5) = 0.7 × 40 = 28
b) Share on X = 0.3 or 30%, Share on Y = 0.7 or 70%
Check: X share = (4 × 15)/200 = 60/200 = 0.3 ✓
Y share = (5 × 28)/200 = 140/200 = 0.7 ✓
c) U = 150.3 × 280.7 ≈ 2.29 × 10.89 ≈ 24.94
Problem 4: For U(X, Y) = XY with I = 100, PX = 5, PY = 10:

a) Find the optimal bundle.
b) What is the MRS at the optimum?
c) Verify that MRS = PX/PY at the optimum.
Solution:
a) MUX = Y, MUY = X
Optimality: Y/5 = X/10 → 2Y = X
Budget: 100 = 5X + 10Y = 5(2Y) + 10Y = 20Y
Y = 5, X = 2(5) = 10
b) MRS = MUX/MUY = Y/X = 5/10 = 0.5
c) PX/PY = 5/10 = 0.5
MRS = PX/PY ✓

Problem 5: A consumer's demand for good X is given by qX = 50 - 2PX + 0.5I, where I is income.

a) Find quantity demanded when PX = 10 and I = 100.
b) Find quantity demanded when PX = 10 and I = 200.
c) Is good X normal or inferior? Explain.
d) Calculate the income elasticity at the midpoint.
Solution:
a) qX = 50 - 2(10) + 0.5(100) = 50 - 20 + 50 = 80
b) qX = 50 - 2(10) + 0.5(200) = 50 - 20 + 100 = 130
c) When I increases from 100 to 200, q increases from 80 to 130.
Good X is a normal good (demand increases with income).
d) EI = (Δq/q) / (ΔI/I)
Δq = 50, average q = 105
ΔI = 100, average I = 150
EI = (50/105) / (100/150) = 0.476 / 0.667 = 0.714
Since 0 < EI < 1, good X is a necessity.
Problem 6: Suppose the price of good X decreases. Decompose the price effect into substitution and income effects for:

a) A normal good
b) An inferior good
Solution:
a) For a normal good when PX decreases:
- Substitution effect: X increases (positive)
- Income effect: X increases (positive)
- Total effect: X increases (SE + IE)

b) For an inferior good when PX decreases:
- Substitution effect: X increases (positive)
- Income effect: X decreases (negative)
- Total effect: Depends on magnitude (SE and IE oppose each other)

Problem 7: Consider the utility function U(X, Y) = X2Y.

a) Find MUX and MUY.
b) Find MRSXY.
c) If PX = 2, PY = 4, and I = 120, find the optimal bundle.

a) MUX = ∂U/∂X = 2XY
MUY = ∂U/∂Y = X2

Problem 8: A consumer spends all her income on two goods, X and Y. At her current consumption bundle, the MRSXY = 3, PX = 6, and PY = 3.

a) Is the consumer maximizing utility? Explain.
b) If not, how should she adjust her consumption to increase utility?

a) At optimum, MRSXY should equal PX/PY.
Current MRS = 3, PX/PY = 6/3 = 2
MRS (3) > PX/PY (2), so NOT at optimum.
b) Interpretation:
MRS = 3 means willing to give up 3 Y for 1 X
PX/PY = 2 means market gives 2 Y for 1 X
Consumer values X more than market does.
Should buy more X and less Y.

Corner vs. Interior Solutions

Interior Solution: Optimal bundle contains positive quantities of all goods. Occurs when MRS can equal the price ratio within the budget set. Typical for well-behaved preferences like Cobb-Douglas.
Corner Solution: Optimal bundle contains zero quantity of at least one good. Occurs when MRS cannot equal the price ratio within the budget set. Typical for perfect substitutes or when one good is dominated.
Example of Corner Solution:
U = 2X + Y, PX = 3, PY = 2
MUX/PX = 2/3 ≈ 0.67
MUY/PY = 1/2 = 0.5
Since MUX/PX > MUY/PY, consumer buys only X (corner solution).

Exam Tips

Key Tips for Consumer Choice Questions:
  1. Identify the utility function type: Is it perfect substitutes, perfect complements, or Cobb-Douglas?
  2. Write down the budget constraint: Always start with I = PXX + PYY
  3. Calculate marginal utilities: Take partial derivatives of U with respect to X and Y
  4. Set up optimality condition: Either MRS = PX/PY or MUX/PX = MUY/PY
  5. Substitute into budget constraint: Solve the system of equations
  6. Check your answer: Verify the solution satisfies both optimality and budget constraint
Common Mistakes to Avoid:
  • Forgetting that MRS is the absolute value of the slope (negative sign is often omitted in practice)
  • Confusing the direction of income effect for inferior goods
  • Not checking for corner solutions in perfect substitutes case
  • Incorrectly calculating marginal utilities for Cobb-Douglas functions
  • Forgetting that substitution effect always opposes price change
Remember:
The Law of Demand holds for all goods except Giffen goods (which are theoretically rare).
Most goods we observe are normal goods.
Inferior goods are less common but do exist.
Giffen goods are primarily a theoretical curiosity.
