Introduction

What has probability got to do with statistics?

  • Probability underlies statistical inference – the drawing of conclusions from a sample of data
  • If samples are drawn at random, their characteristics (such as the sample mean) depend upon chance
  • Hence, to understand how to interpret sample evidence, we need to understand chance, or probability
  • Besides, probability is fun! Most people have a poor intuitive grasp of probability so questions are often thought-provoking

What is the probability of an event?

Frequentist view:

The proportion of trials in which an outcome occurs, calculated as the number of trials approaches infinity.

Subjective view:

Someone's subjective degree of belief about the likelihood of an outcome occurring. However, you should update your beliefs in the light of evidence.

Definitions of terms

  • An experiment: an activity such as tossing a coin, which has a range of possible outcomes
  • A trial: a single performance of the experiment
  • The sample space: all possible outcomes of the experiment.
    • For a single toss of a coin the sample space is {Heads, Tails}
    • For rolling a die it is {1, 2, …, 6}
    • The outcomes are mutually exclusive – only one can arise from a single trial. You cannot get both heads and tails by tossing a coin!
  • An event: a combination of outcomes, for example:
    • Rolling a die and obtaining an even number
    • A roulette wheel ball landing on one of the red numbers

Rules for probabilities

A probability is represented by a number. But there are restrictions on the numbers that can be used.

  • 0 ≤ Pr(A) ≤ 1
  • ∑i pi = 1, summed over all outcomes
  • Pr(not-A) = 1 − Pr(A)

The complement of event A

Pr(not-A) = 1 - Pr(A)

Example – picking a card from a pack

  • The probability of picking any one card from a pack (e.g. King of Spades) is 1/52. This is same for each card
  • 0 ≤ Pr(King of Spades) ≤ 1
  • Summing over all cards: 1/52 + 1/52 + ... = 1
  • Pr(not-King of Spades) = 51/52 = 1 − Pr(King of Spades)
∑i pi = 1

Rules for calculating probabilities of compound events

1. The "or" rule: adding probabilities

Pr(A or B) = Pr(A) + Pr(B)

Example 1:

The probability of rolling a five or six on a single roll of a die is:

Pr(5 or 6) = Pr(5) + Pr(6) = 1/6 + 1/6 = 2/6 = 1/3

Example 2:

Pr(any King) from a deck of cards:

1/52 + 1/52 + 1/52 + 1/52 = 4/52 = 1/13

Sample space: {1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6}

… and solution

Subtract (once) the double counted King of Hearts:

Pr(King or Heart) = 4/52 + 13/52 - 1/52 = 16/52

The general formula:

Pr(A or B) = Pr(A) + Pr(B) - Pr(A and B)

When A and B cannot occur simultaneously, Pr(A and B) = 0, and the rule simplifies to:

Pr(A or B) = Pr(A) + Pr(B)

Second rule for combining probabilities

The "and" rule: multiply probabilities

Pr(A and B) = Pr(A) × Pr(B)

Example:

The probability of obtaining a double-six when rolling two dice is:

Pr(Six and Six) = Pr(Six) × Pr(Six) = 1/6 × 1/6 = 1/36

Another complication! Independence

Example: Drawing two Aces from a pack of cards, without replacement

  • If first card drawn is an Ace (P = 4/52), that leaves 51 cards, of which 3 are Aces
  • The probability of drawing second Ace is 3/51, different from the probability of drawing the first Ace
  • They are not independent events. The probability changes!
Pr(two Aces) = 4/52 × 3/51 = 12/2652 = 1/221

Solution… conditional probability

3/51 is the probability of drawing an Ace given that an Ace was drawn as the first card.

This is called conditional probability and is written as:

Pr(Second Ace | Ace on first draw)

To simplify notation, write as Pr(A₂|A₁)

This is the probability of event A₂ occurring, given that A₁ has occurred.

Recap of rules

Addition rule ("or" rule):

Pr(A or B) = Pr(A) + Pr(B) – Pr(A and B)

If mutually exclusive, then Pr(A and B) = 0 and rule simplifies to:

Pr(A or B) = Pr(A) + Pr(B)

Multiplication rule ("and" rule):

Pr(A and B) = Pr(A) × Pr(B|A)

If independent, Pr(B|A) = Pr(B) and the rule simplifies to:

Pr(A and B) = Pr(A) × Pr(B)

Conditional Probability — When Our Probabilities Depend on Other Events

Example: Breast Cancer Screening

When screening for breast cancer, mammography is roughly 90% accurate, in the sense that:

  • 90% of women with cancer will be correctly classified (positive test)
  • 90% of women without cancer will be correctly classified (negative test)

Suppose 1% of women being screened actually have cancer. What is:

  1. The probability that a randomly chosen woman will have a positive mammogram?
  2. If she does, what is the chance she really has cancer?

Understanding the problem

In the two-coins case, events were independent, in that the probability of flipping a head on the second flip did not depend on what the first flip was.

This example is somewhat more relevant to real life!

This kind of problem is a classic in intelligence tests and is not an easy problem to solve, but using the idea of expected frequency it becomes remarkably straightforward.

The crucial idea is to think of what we would expect to happen to a large group of women, say 1,000, as shown in the diagram.

Results and Interpretation

  • Out of 1,000 women, 10 (1%) actually have breast cancer
  • Of these 10, 9 (90%) have a positive result
  • But out of 990 women without cancer, 99 (10%) are falsely given a positive mammography result

Putting these together makes a total of 9 + 99 = 108 positive mammograms

Key Findings:

  • Probability of a positive result: 108/1,000 = 10.8%
  • But of these 108 with positive results, only 9 truly have cancer
  • Probability that a woman with a positive test actually has cancer: 9/108 = 8.3%

Counter-intuitive results

Important Lessons:

This exercise in conditional probability helps us understand a very counter-intuitive result: in spite of the "90% accuracy" of the scan, the vast majority of women with a positive mammogram do not have breast cancer.

  • It is easy to confuse the probability of a positive test, given cancer, with the probability of cancer, given a positive test.
  • Pr(Positive | Cancer) = 90%
  • Pr(Cancer | Positive) ≈ 8%

This type of confusion is popularly known as the prosecutor's fallacy, since it is so prevalent in court cases involving DNA.

The Prosecutor's Fallacy Explained

DNA Evidence Example:

A forensic expert might claim, for example, that "if the accused is innocent, there is only a 1 in a billion chance that they would match the DNA found at the crime scene."

But this is wrongly interpreted as meaning: "given the DNA evidence, there is only a 1 in a billion chance that the accused is innocent."

The Logical Flaw:

It is an easy mistake to make, but the logic is as faulty as going from the statement:

"If you're the Pope, then you're a Catholic"

to the statement:

"If you're a Catholic, then you're the Pope"

where the flaw is somewhat simpler to spot!

The combinatorial formula

The combination formula:

The combinatorial formula gives the number of routes through the diagram:

nCr = n! / [r! × (n - r)!]

where:

  • n = total number of items
  • r = number of items to choose

Example:

From 5 elements {A, B, C, D, E}, choose 3 (in any order):

5C3 = 5! / [3! × (5-3)!] = 5! / (3! × 2!) = 10

The 10 combinations are:

1. ABC2. ABD
3. ABE4. ACD
5. ACE6. ADE
7. BCD8. BDE
9. BDF10. CDE

nCr gives the number of ways of choosing r elements (in any order) from a set of n elements.

The combinatorial formula (continued)

We can write the probability of 1 Head in 2 tosses as:

Pr(2 Heads) = ½ × ½ × 2C1 = ¼ × 2 = ½

We can formalise this in the Binomial distribution...

  • the number of possible orderings = nCr
  • the probability of a head and a tail (in that order) = ½ × ½
Pr(1 Head in 2 tosses) = ½ × ½ × 2C1 = 1/2

The Binomial Distribution

Binomial Distribution Formula:

If we define P as the probability of Heads, and hence (1 – P) is the probability of Tails, we can write:

Pr(r Heads) = nCr × Pr × (1 - P)n-r

This is the formula for the Binomial distribution. It summarises the calculation to use in all probability problems of this type.

It gives the probability of obtaining r heads and only requires information about the two parameters, n and P.

Binomial Distribution - Example

Calculate P(3 heads in 5 tosses):

  • n = 5
  • r = 3
  • P = ½
Pr(3 Heads) = 5C3 × (½)3 × (½)2

Step by step calculation:

5C3 = 5! / (3! × 2!) = 10
(½)3 = 1/8
(½)2 = 1/4
Pr(3 Heads) = 10 × 1/8 × 1/4 = 10/32 = 5/16 = 0.3125

Appendix: Why does the combinatorial formula work?

Example: Three girls in five births

Three girls to fill three of five places. Let's name them: Amanda, Bridget, Caroline.

  • For Amanda, there is a choice of five places
  • For Bridget, there is a choice of four places
  • For Caroline, there is a choice of three places

There are 5 × 4 × 3 = 60 possible orderings (two boys take the remaining two places).

60 is the number of permutations of three named girls in five births. This is written as:

5P3 = 5! / (5-3)! = 60

General Permutation Formula

Permutation Formula:

nPr = n × (n-1) × ... × (n-r+1) = n! / (n-r)!

A simpler formula can be obtained by dividing and multiplying by (n-r)!

nPr = nCr × r!
Page 34 of 40
Copyright © 2018, 2013, 2009 Pearson Education, Inc. All Rights Reserved

What is the difference between nPr and nCr?

Combination does not distinguish between the girls: Two cases {A, B, C, boy, boy} and {B, A, C, boy, boy} are the same.

So nPr is larger than nCr by a factor of the number of ways of ordering the r chosen items.

This factor is given by r! = r × (r-1) × ... × 1 = 3 × 2 × 1 = 6 (for r=3).

nPr = nCr × r!

Example: Probability of three girls and two boys in a family of five children?

The different arrangements are:

GGGBB; GGBGB; GGBBG; GBGGB; GBGBG;

GBBGG; BGGGB; BGGBG; BGBGG; BBGGG.

That's 10 different arrangements, which equals 5C3.

Probability = 10 × (½)5 = 10/32 = 5/16

Bayes' Theorem Solution

Bayes' Theorem Formula:

Pr(A|R) = [Pr(R|A) × Pr(A)] / Pr(R)

We require Pr(A|R). This can be written:

Pr(A|R) = [Pr(R|A) × Pr(A)] / [Pr(R|A) × Pr(A) + Pr(R|B) × Pr(B)]

Similarly, Pr(B|R) can be calculated:

Pr(B|R) = [Pr(R|B) × Pr(B)] / [Pr(R|A) × Pr(A) + Pr(R|B) × Pr(B)]

Lessons

Summary of Key Concepts:

  1. Probability theory builds upon simple foundations – probability of an outcome, complement, etc.
  2. There are basic rules to avoid contradictions – 0 ≤ P ≤ 1, for example
  3. More complex events can be addressed using addition and multiplication rules for probabilities
  4. Independence of events simplifies rules and any analysis
  5. These rules lead on to probability distributions such as the Binomial
  6. Bayes' theorem tells us how to update probabilities in light of evidence

Example

On how many ways can the letters of the word "COMPUTER" be arranged such that Vowels occupy the even positions (the vowels can occupy only even positions)?

Analysis of the Problem:

The word "COMPUTER" has 8 letters. There are 5 consonants and 3 vowels in it.

Writing in the following way makes it easier to solve these types of questions:

(1)(2)(3)(4)(5)(6)(7)(8)
Appendix Page 1 of 2

Solution - Step by Step

Step 1: There are 4 positions available for Vowels (positions 2, 4, 6, 8).

The number of ways 3 vowels can occur in 4 different places is:

4P3 = 4! / (4-3)! = 4! = 24 ways

Step 2: After 3 vowels take 3 places, the number of ways 5 consonants can take the 5 remaining places is:

5P5 = 5! = 120 ways

Step 3: Therefore, the total number of possible permutations is:

24 × 120 = 2,880 ways
