Lecture 1: Consumer Choice Theory

Consumer choice theory is a fundamental concept in microeconomics that explains how individuals allocate their limited income across various goods and services to maximize their satisfaction or utility.

Overview

  • Consumers make choices subject to budget constraints
  • Choices are guided by preferences over different bundles of goods
  • The goal is to maximize utility (satisfaction)
  • We use indifference curves to represent preferences
  • We use budget lines to represent constraints

Key Assumptions of Consumer Theory

  1. Rationality: Consumers prefer more to less and can consistently rank bundles
  2. Completeness: Consumers can compare any two bundles
  3. Transitivity: If A > B and B > C, then A > C
  4. Non-satiation: More is always better (at least never worse)
  5. Convexity: Consumers prefer averages to extremes

Utility Functions

Utility Function - A mathematical representation that assigns a numerical value to each possible consumption bundle, representing the level of satisfaction derived from that bundle.
U = f(X, Y, Z, ...)

where X, Y, Z represent quantities of different goods.

Types of Utility

Cardinal Utility

Utility can be measured in absolute units (utils). This approach is less commonly used in modern economics.

Problem: Utility is subjective and cannot be objectively measured. Saying one bundle gives "10 utils" and another gives "20 utils" implies meaningful differences that may not exist.

Ordinal Utility

Utility can only be ranked (preferences can be ordered, but not measured). This is the approach used in indifference curve analysis.

Key Point: With ordinal utility, we only need to know if one bundle is preferred to another, not "how much more."

Properties of Utility Functions

1. Completeness

Consumers can compare any two bundles (X₁, Y₁) and (X₂, Y₂) and determine which is preferred or if they are indifferent.

For any two bundles A and B, exactly one is true:

  • A is preferred to B (A > B)
  • B is preferred to A (B > A)
  • Consumer is indifferent between A and B (A ~ B)
2. Transitivity

If bundle A is preferred to B, and B is preferred to C, then A must be preferred to C.

Formally: If A > B and B > C, then A > C

Importance: Without transitivity, we could have circular preferences (A > B > C > A), which makes choice impossible.
Page 3
3. Non-satiation (Monotonicity)

Consumers prefer more of any good to less, ceteris paribus.

If (X₁, Y₁) > (X₂, Y₂) whenever X₁ > X₂ and Y₁ > Y₂

This implies:

  • Indifference curves are negatively sloped
  • Higher indifference curves represent higher utility
  • Consumers always want to reach higher curves
4. Convexity

Consumers prefer average bundles to extreme ones (diminishing marginal rate of substitution).

If (X₁, Y₁) ~ (X₂, Y₂), then the average bundle ((X₁+X₂)/2, (Y₁+Y₂)/2) is preferred to either original bundle.

Economic Interpretation: This reflects the idea of diminishing marginal utility and variety preference. A mix of goods often provides more satisfaction than extremes.

Marginal Utility

Marginal Utility (MU) - The additional utility from consuming one more unit of a good.
MUX = ∂U/∂X

where ∂ is the partial derivative symbol, indicating change in utility with respect to one good holding others constant.

Law of Diminishing Marginal Utility

Law of Diminishing Marginal Utility

As consumption of a good increases, the marginal utility from each additional unit decreases, holding consumption of other goods constant.

∂²U/∂X² < 0
Numerical Example:
Units Consumed Total Utility Marginal Utility
1 10 10
2 18 8
3 24 6
4 28 4
5 30 2

Indifference Curves

Indifference Curve - A curve showing all combinations of two goods that yield the same level of utility.
U(X, Y) = U₀

where U₀ is a constant level of utility.

Properties of Indifference Curves

  1. Negative Slope: To maintain constant utility, increasing X requires decreasing Y.
  2. Convex to Origin: MRS decreases as X increases (diminishing MRS).
  3. No Intersection: Indifference curves cannot cross (transitivity).
  4. Higher = Better: Curves further from origin represent higher utility.
Figure 1: Indifference Curve

Marginal Rate of Substitution (MRS)

Marginal Rate of Substitution (MRSXY) - The rate at which a consumer is willing to trade Y for X while staying on the same indifference curve.
MRSXY = -dY/dX = MUX/MUY

The MRS is the absolute value of the slope of the indifference curve at any point.

Understanding MRS

Interpretation:

If MRSXY = 3:

  • Consumer is willing to give up 3 units of Y
  • In exchange for 1 additional unit of X
  • While maintaining the same level of utility
Diminishing MRS

As a consumer consumes more of good X and less of good Y, the marginal rate of substitution of X for Y decreases.

dMRSXY/dX < 0
Why? As you have more X, its marginal utility decreases (law of diminishing MU). As you have less Y, its marginal utility increases. Therefore, you're less willing to trade Y for X.

Budget Constraint

Budget Constraint - All combinations of goods a consumer can purchase given income and prices.
I = PXX + PYY

where:

  • I = Consumer's income
  • PX, PY = Prices of goods X and Y
  • X, Y = Quantities of goods X and Y

Budget Line Characteristics

Characteristic Formula Interpretation
Y-intercept I/PY Maximum Y when X = 0
X-intercept I/PX Maximum X when Y = 0
Slope -PX/PY Opportunity cost of X in terms of Y
Slope = -PX/PY
Key Insight: The slope of the budget line represents the rate at which the market allows trading Y for X. The MRS represents the rate at which the consumer is willing to trade. At optimum, these two rates must be equal.

Y I/PX Affordable Region Slope = -PX/PY

Budget Set

Budget Set - The set of all affordable bundles given income and prices.
B = {(X, Y) | PXX + PYY ≤ I, X ≥ 0, Y ≥ 0}

The budget set is the triangle (or higher-dimensional simplex) bounded by:

  • The budget line
  • The axes (X ≥ 0 and Y ≥ 0)

Consumer Optimization

The consumer's problem is to maximize utility subject to the budget constraint.

The Consumer's Optimization Problem:
Max U(X, Y) subject to I = PXX + PYY

Graphical Solution: Tangency Condition

Optimality Condition:
MRSXY = PX/PY

At the optimal bundle:

  • The indifference curve is tangent to the budget line
  • Consumer reaches the highest possible indifference curve within the budget constraint
1 U2 U3 E* (optimum)
Figure 3: Consumer Optimum (Tangency Point)

Algebraic Interpretation of Optimality

The tangency condition MRS = PX/PY can also be expressed as:

MUX/PX = MUY/PY

Interpretation:

  • MUX/PX = Marginal utility per dollar spent on X
  • MUY/PY = Marginal utility per dollar spent on Y

At optimum, marginal utility per dollar spent must be equal across all goods.

Example:

If MUX/PX > MUY/PY:

  • Consumer gets more utility from last dollar spent on X than Y
  • Should buy more X, less Y to increase utility

This continues until MUX/PX = MUY/PY

Proof of Optimality Condition:

At the optimal bundle (X*, Y*):

  1. The bundle lies on the budget line: PXX* + PYY* = I
  2. The indifference curve is tangent to budget line: |slope of IC| = |slope of BL|
  3. MRS = |dY/dX| = |-PX/PY| = PX/PY
Page 11

Lagrangian Method

The constrained optimization problem can be solved algebraically using the Lagrangian method.

Setting up the Lagrangian

ℒ(X, Y, λ) = U(X, Y) + λ(I - PXX - PYY)

where λ (lambda) is the Lagrange multiplier, representing the marginal utility of income.

First-Order Conditions (FOCs)

∂ℒ/∂X = MUX - λPX = 0
∂ℒ/∂Y = MUY - λPY = 0
∂ℒ/∂λ = I - PXX - PYY = 0

The third condition is simply the budget constraint.

Solving the System

From the first two conditions:

MUX/PX = MUY/PY = λ

This gives us the optimal ratio of goods. Combined with the budget constraint, we can solve for X and Y.

Page 12

Interpretation of the Lagrange Multiplier (λ)

Lagrange Multiplier (λ) - The shadow price of the budget constraint. It represents the marginal utility of income.
λ = ∂U*/∂I

where U* is the maximum utility at the optimal bundle.

Interpretation:

If λ = 2 at the optimum:

  • An additional $1 of income would increase maximum utility by approximately 2 utils
  • The consumer values an extra dollar of income at 2 utils

Properties of λ

  • λ > 0 for standard problems with non-satiation
  • λ decreases as income increases (diminishing marginal utility of income)
  • λ is the same for all goods (by the optimality condition)
  • λ is also called the "marginal utility of money"
Important: The Lagrange multiplier has a clear economic interpretation—it tells us how much utility would increase if we relaxed the budget constraint by one unit (gave the consumer one more dollar).

Income and Price Effects

Income Effect

A change in income shifts the budget line parallel to itself:

  • Increase in income: Budget line shifts outward (consumer can afford more)
  • Decrease in income: Budget line shifts inward (consumer can afford less)

The slope remains unchanged because prices (and therefore the price ratio) are constant.

Figure 4: Income Effect on Budget Line
Page 14

Price Effect

A change in the price of one good rotates the budget line around the intercept of the other good:

  • Decrease in PX: Budget line rotates outward around the Y-intercept
  • Increase in PX: Budget line rotates inward around the Y-intercept
  • Decrease in PY: Budget line rotates outward around the X-intercept
  • Increase in PY: Budget line rotates inward around the X-intercept

The intercept of the good whose price hasn't changed remains the same.

Decomposition of Price Effect

The total effect of a price change can be decomposed into two effects:

Price Effect = Substitution Effect + Income Effect

Substitution Effect (SE)

The change in consumption due to the change in relative prices, holding utility constant.

  • The consumer substitutes toward the now cheaper good
  • Always moves in the opposite direction of the price change
  • If PX falls: SE increases consumption of X
  • If PX rises: SE decreases consumption of X

Income Effect (IE)

The change in consumption due to the change in purchasing power.

Good Type Direction of IE Relationship to SE
Normal Good Positive (more income, more consumption) Reinforces SE
Inferior Good Negative (more income, less consumption) Opposes SE
Page 16

Normal vs. Inferior Goods

Normal Goods

Normal Good - A good for which demand increases when income increases (and decreases when income decreases).
  • Income elasticity of demand is positive: EI > 0
  • Income effect reinforces substitution effect
  • When PX falls: SE increases X, IE increases X → Total effect = increase in X

Inferior Goods

Inferior Good - A good for which demand decreases when income increases (and increases when income decreases).
  • Income elasticity of demand is negative: EI < 0
  • Income effect works against substitution effect
  • When PX falls: SE increases X, IE decreases X → Total effect depends on relative magnitudes
Examples:
Normal Goods Inferior Goods
Restaurant meals Instant noodles
New cars Used clothing
Electronics Public transportation
Page 17

Giffen Goods

Giffen Good - A special type of inferior good where the negative income effect is so strong that it outweighs the substitution effect, resulting in a positive relationship between price and quantity demanded (violating the law of demand).

Characteristics of Giffen Goods

  • Must be an inferior good
  • Must take a large portion of consumer's budget
  • Must have few close substitutes
  • Creates an upward-sloping demand curve

Price Effect for Giffen Goods

When PX rises:

  • SE: Decreases X (negative)
  • IE: Increases X (positive, because real income falls)
  • If |IE| > |SE|: Net effect = Increase in X

This violates the law of demand (price up, quantity down).

Classic Example: The Irish potato famine. When potato prices rose, poor people could afford less meat and had to buy more potatoes to survive. Potatoes were an inferior good taking a large portion of their budget with few substitutes.
Important Note: Giffen goods are primarily theoretical. They have been observed in rare historical cases but are not commonly found in modern markets.

Special Cases of Preferences

1. Perfect Substitutes

Perfect Substitutes - Goods that can replace each other at a constant rate. The consumer is always willing to substitute one good for another at a fixed ratio.
U(X, Y) = aX + bY
MRSXY = a/b (constant)

Characteristics:

  • Indifference curves are straight, parallel lines
  • MRS is constant along each indifference curve
  • Corner solution: Consumer buys only the good with lower marginal utility per dollar
1 U2 U3 Corner solution
Figure 6: Perfect Substitutes
Page 19

2. Perfect Complements

Perfect Complements - Goods that are consumed together in fixed proportions. The consumer gains no additional utility from having more of one good without having a proportional increase in the other.
U(X, Y) = min{aX, bY}

Characteristics:

  • Indifference curves are L-shaped
  • The corner of each L is at the fixed ratio a:b
  • MRS is undefined at the corner and 0 or ∞ elsewhere
  • Corner solution: Consumer always consumes at the kink (fixed ratio)
Figure 7: Perfect Complements
Page 20

3. Cobb-Douglas Preferences

Cobb-Douglas Utility Function - A widely used form of utility function with well-behaved properties (convex, smooth indifference curves).
U(X, Y) = XαYβ

where α, β > 0. Often we normalize so that α + β = 1.

Marginal Utilities for Cobb-Douglas

MUX = ∂U/∂X = αXα-1Yβ = α(Y/X)
MUY = ∂U/∂Y = βXαYβ-1 = β(X/Y)

MRS for Cobb-Douglas

MRSXY = MUX/MUY = (α/β)(Y/X)

Demand Functions for Cobb-Douglas

X = (α/(α + β)) × (I/PX)
Y = (β/(α + β)) × (I/PY)
Key Property: For Cobb-Douglas utility, the consumer spends a constant share of income on each good. Share on X = α/(α + β), share on Y = β/(α + β).
Example: For U = X0.3Y0.7:
Consumer spends 30% of income on X and 70% on Y, regardless of prices.
Page 21

Corner vs. Interior Solutions

Interior Solutions

Interior Solution - Optimal bundle contains positive quantities of all goods. Occurs when MRS can equal the price ratio within the budget set. Typical for well-behaved preferences like Cobb-Douglas.

At an interior solution:

  • X* > 0, Y* > 0
  • MRSXY = PX/PY
  • MUX/PX = MUY/PY

Corner Solutions

Corner Solution - Optimal bundle contains zero quantity of at least one good. Occurs when MRS cannot equal the price ratio within the budget set.

At a corner solution:

  • Either X* = 0 or Y* = 0 (or both)
  • MRS does not equal PX/PY
  • Typical for perfect substitutes or when one good is dominated
Corner Solution Example:
U = 2X + Y, PX = 3, PY = 2
MUX/PX = 2/3 ≈ 0.67
MUY/PY = 1/2 = 0.5
Since MUX/PX > MUY/PY, consumer buys only X (corner solution).

Practice Problems

Problem 1: Given the utility function U(X, Y) = 3X + 2Y, find:
a) MUX and MUY
b) MRSXY
c) Is this a case of perfect substitutes?
Solution:
a) MUX = ∂U/∂X = 3
MUY = ∂U/∂Y = 2
b) MRSXY = MUX/MUY = 3/2 = 1.5 (constant)
c) Yes, this is a perfect substitutes utility function (linear form with constant MRS).
Problem 2: Given the utility function U(X, Y) = min{2X, 4Y}, find:
a) What type of preferences does this represent?
b) If consumer has 10 units of X and 3 units of Y, what is utility?
c) What is the optimal consumption ratio?
Solution:
a) Perfect complements (Leontief preferences).
b) Utility = min{2(10), 4(3)} = min{20, 12} = 12
c) At optimum: 2X = 4Y, so X = 2Y (consume in ratio 2:1).
Problem 3: For U(X, Y) = X0.5Y0.5 with I = 100, PX = 4, PY = 5:
a) Find MUX, MUY, and MRSXY
b) Find the optimal quantities of X and Y.
Page 24
Solution to Problem 3 (continued):
a) For Cobb-Douglas with α = β = 0.5:
MUX = 0.5X-0.5Y0.5 = 0.5(Y/X)0.5
MUY = 0.5X0.5Y-0.5 = 0.5(X/Y)0.5
MRSXY = (0.5/0.5)(Y/X) = Y/X
b) For Cobb-Douglas with α = β:
X = (0.5/1) × (100/4) = 0.5 × 25 = 12.5
Y = (0.5/1) × (100/5) = 0.5 × 20 = 10
Optimal bundle: (12.5, 10)
Verify budget: 4(12.5) + 5(10) = 50 + 50 = 100 ✓
Problem 4: A consumer's budget line is given by 100 = 5X + 10Y.
a) What is the maximum quantity of X the consumer can buy?
b) What is the maximum quantity of Y the consumer can buy?
c) What is the slope of the budget line?
d) If the consumer buys 6 units of X, how many units of Y can they buy?
Solution:
a) Maximum X (when Y = 0): 100 = 5X → X = 20
b) Maximum Y (when X = 0): 100 = 10Y → Y = 10
c) Slope = -PX/PY = -5/10 = -0.5
d) If X = 6: 100 = 5(6) + 10Y = 30 + 10Y → 10Y = 70 → Y = 7
Problem 5: Explain the economic meaning of the Lagrange multiplier λ in consumer optimization.
Page 25
Solution to Problem 5:

The Lagrange multiplier λ represents the marginal utility of income. It measures how much additional utility a consumer would gain from an additional unit of income, assuming optimal consumption.

Interpretation:

  • λ = ∂U*/∂I: The derivative of maximum utility with respect to income
  • If λ = 3, gaining $1 more would increase maximum utility by approximately 3 utils
  • λ is higher for poorer consumers (diminishing marginal utility of income)
  • λ is also called the "shadow price" of the budget constraint
Problem 6: For a consumer with U(X, Y) = XY, income I = 80, and prices PX = 4, PY = 8:
a) Set up the Lagrangian.
b) Write down the first-order conditions.
c) Solve for the optimal bundle.
Solution:
a) ℒ = XY + λ(80 - 4X - 8Y)
b) First-order conditions:
∂ℒ/∂X = Y - 4λ = 0
∂ℒ/∂Y = X - 8λ = 0
∂ℒ/∂λ = 80 - 4X - 8Y = 0
c) From first two: Y = 4λ and X = 8λ, so X = 2Y
From budget: 80 = 4(2Y) + 8Y = 16Y → Y = 5, X = 10
Optimal bundle: (10, 5)

Problem 8: A consumer spends exactly half her income on good X and half on good Y. What does this imply about her utility function? Assuming PX = PY.
Solution:

If PX = PY = P, and consumer spends equal amounts on both goods:

Expenditure on X: PX = 0.5I → X = 0.5I/P

Expenditure on Y: PY = 0.5I → Y = 0.5I/P

This implies a Cobb-Douglas utility function with equal exponents: U = X0.5Y0.5

For Cobb-Douglas, the expenditure share on each good equals the exponent share. Since shares are equal (0.5 each), exponents are equal (0.5 each).

Problem 9: Explain why the substitution effect always moves in the opposite direction of the price change.
Page 27
Solution to Problem 9:

The substitution effect always moves in the opposite direction of the price change because:

  1. Relative price change: When the price of good X falls, good X becomes relatively cheaper compared to good Y.
  2. Substitution behavior: The rational consumer substitutes toward the now cheaper good (X) and away from the relatively more expensive good (Y).
  3. Utility maintenance: The substitution effect is defined as the change in consumption while holding utility constant, isolating the pure substitution behavior.
Key Insight: The substitution effect is purely about changing consumption patterns due to changed relative prices, without any consideration of changed purchasing power. Therefore, it always responds negatively to a price increase (buy less) and positively to a price decrease (buy more).
Problem 10: For U(X, Y) = X0.4Y0.6 with I = 120, PX = 6, PY = 4:
a) What percentage of income is spent on X?
b) What percentage of income is spent on Y?
c) Find the optimal bundle.
Solution:

For Cobb-Douglas with α = 0.4, β = 0.6:

a) Share on X = α/(α+β) = 0.4/1 = 40%

b) Share on Y = β/(α+β) = 0.6/1 = 60%

c) X = 0.4 × (120/6) = 0.4 × 20 = 8

Y = 0.6 × (120/4) = 0.6 × 30 = 18

Optimal bundle: (8, 18)

Verify: 6(8) + 4(18) = 48 + 72 = 120 ✓

Key Concepts Summary

Consumer Choice Theory Core Concepts:
  • Utility: Measure of satisfaction from consumption
  • Marginal Utility: Additional utility from one more unit
  • Law of Diminishing MU: MU decreases as consumption increases
  • Indifference Curves: Bundles giving same utility
  • MRS: Rate willing to trade Y for X
  • Budget Constraint: All affordable bundles
  • Consumer Optimum: MRS = PX/PY
  • Income Effect: Change due to purchasing power
  • Substitution Effect: Change due to relative prices
  • Price Effect: SE + IE
  • Normal Good: Demand increases with income
  • Inferior Good: Demand decreases with income
  • Giffen Good: Inferior good where IE overcomes SE
  • Perfect Substitutes: Constant MRS (linear preferences)
  • Perfect Complements: L-shaped ICs (fixed ratios)
  • Cobb-Douglas: Well-behaved convex preferences
Remember:
  • Substitution effect always opposes the price change
  • Income effect direction depends on normal/inferior classification
  • For normal goods: SE and IE work together
  • For inferior goods: SE and IE oppose each other
  • Giffen goods are primarily theoretical
