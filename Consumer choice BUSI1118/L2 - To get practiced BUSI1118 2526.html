Problem Set 1: Individual Demand Derivation

Exercise 1.1: A consumer has utility function U(X, Y) = X0.5Y0.5 and income I = $200. The price of good Y is PY = $5.

a) Find the demand function for good X, qX(PX).
b) Find the quantity of X demanded when PX = $10.
Solution:

For a Cobb-Douglas utility function U = XαYβ, the demand functions are:

X = (α/(α + β)) · (I/PX)
Y = (β/(α + β)) · (I/PY)

Here, α = 0.5 and β = 0.5, so α/(α + β) = 0.5/1 = 0.5.


a) Demand for X: qX(PX) = 0.5 × (200/PX) = 100/PX


b) When PX = $10: qX = 100/10 = 10 units.

Exercise 1.2: Using the demand function from Exercise 1.1 (qX = 100/PX):
  1. Find the quantity demanded when PX = $5, $10, and $20.
  2. Calculate the own-price elasticity of demand at PX = $10.
  3. Classify the good based on the elasticity value.
Solution:

Given: qX = 100/PX


a) Quantities demanded:

  • When PX = $5: qX = 100/5 = 20
  • When PX = $10: qX = 100/10 = 10
  • When PX = $20: qX = 100/20 = 5

b) Own-price elasticity EXX at PX = $10:

EXX = (dq/dP) × (P/Q) = (-100/PX2) × (PX/qX)

For q = 100/PX: dq/dP = -100/PX2

EXX = (-100/PX2) × PX/q = (-100/PX2) × PX/(100/PX) = (-100/PX2) × PX2/100 = -1

c) Since EXX = -1 (unit elastic in absolute value), demand is unit elastic. A 1% increase in price leads to a 1% decrease in quantity demanded.

Exercise 1.3: A consumer has demand function q = 50 - 2P.

a) Calculate the price elasticity of demand at P = 5, P = 10, and P = 20.
  • Find the price at which total revenue is maximized.
  • What is the maximum total revenue?
    • Solution:

    Given: q = 50 - 2P


    a) Price elasticities:

    Total Revenue TR = P × q = P(50 - 2P) = 50P - 2P2

    dTR/dP = 50 - 4P

    At P = 5: q = 50 - 2(5) = 40, E = (dq/dP)(P/q) = (-2)(5/40) = -0.25

    At P = 10: q = 50 - 2(10) = 30, E = (-2)(10/30) = -0.667

    At P = 20: q = 50 - 2(20) = 10, E = (-2)(20/10) = -4


    b) To find the revenue-maximizing price:

    Set dTR/dP = 0 → 50 - 4P = 0 → P = 50/4 = $12.50

    c) Maximum total revenue:

    At P = 12.50, q = 50 - 2(12.50) = 25

    TR = 12.50 × 25 = $312.50

    Verification: At unit elastic demand, maximum revenue occurs at the midpoint of the demand curve where E = -1.

    Exercise 1.4: Consider two goods, X and Y, with the following demand functions:

    qX = 60 - 2PX
    qY = 40 - PY

    a) Find the cross-price elasticity of demand for good X with respect to good Y at PX = 10 and PY = 5.
  • b) Classify the relationship between goods X and Y based on the cross-price elasticity.
    • Solution:

    a) Cross-price elasticity of demand EXY = (%ΔqX/qX) / (%ΔPY/PY)

    Using point elasticity formula (evaluated at the point):

    EXY = (∂qX/∂PY) × (PY/qX)

    From qX = 60 - 2PX: ∂qX/∂PY = -2

    At PX = 10, PY = 5: qX = 60 - 2(10) = 40

    EXY = (-2) × (5/40) = -10/40 = -0.25

    b) Classification:

    • Since EXY = -0.25 < 0, goods X and Y are complements.
    • The negative sign indicates that an increase in the price of Y reduces the quantity demanded of X.
    • When PY rises, consumers buy less of both goods, including X.

    Exercise 1.5: For a consumer with utility function U(X, Y) = XY, income I = 100, and prices PX = 5, PY = 5:

    a) Find the optimal bundle.
  • b) Calculate the price elasticity of demand for good X at the optimum.
  • c) Calculate the income elasticity of demand for good X at the optimum.
    • Solution:

    a) Find the optimal bundle:

    For U = XY: MUX = Y, MUY = X

    Optimality condition: MUX/PX = MUY/PY

    Y/5 = X/5 → Y = X

    Budget constraint: 100 = 5X + 5Y = 5X + 5X = 10X

    X = 10, Y = 10

    Optimal bundle: (10, 10)


    b) Price elasticity of demand for good X at the optimum:

    At the optimum (X = 10, Y = 10):

    Using the derived demand function for Cobb-Douglas with α = β = 0.5:

    qX = 0.5 × (I/PX) = I/(2PX)

    Point price elasticity:

    EXX = (dq/dP) × (P/q) = (-I/(2PX2)) × P/q

    At PX = 5, I = 100, X = 10:

    EXX = (-100/(2 × 25)) × 5/10 = -2 × 0.5 = -1

    Unit elastic demand (|E| = 1).


    c) Income elasticity of demand:

    For Cobb-Douglas with α = 0.5:

    qX = 0.5 × (I/PX)

    Income elasticity:

    EI = (dq/dI) × (I/q) = 0.5 × (1/PX) × (I/q)

    At I = 100, PX = 5, X = 10:

    EI = 0.5 × (1/5) × (5/10) = 0.1 × 0.5 = 0.5

    Normal good (EI > 0) with EI = 0.5 indicating it's a necessity (0 < E < 1).

    Exercise 1.6: A consumer's demand for good X is given by qX = 50 - 2P + 0.5I, where I is income.

    a) Find quantity demanded when PX = 10 and I = 100.
  • b) Find quantity demanded when PX = 10 and I = 200.
  • c) Is good X normal or inferior? Explain.
  • d) Calculate the income elasticity at the midpoint.
    • Solution:

    Given: qX = 50 - 2P + 0.5I


    a) When P = 10, I = 100:

    qX = 50 - 2(10) + 0.5(100) = 50 - 20 + 50 = 80

    b) When P = 10, I = 200:

    qX = 50 - 2(10) + 0.5(200) = 50 - 20 + 100 = 130

    c) Classification:

    When income increases from 100 to 200, quantity demanded increases from 80 to 130. This indicates that good X is a normal good.

    For a normal good, demand moves in the same direction as income changes.


    d) Income elasticity at midpoint (arc formula):

    Imid = (100 + 200)/2 = 150

    qmid = (80 + 130)/2 = 105

    ΔI = 200 - 100 = 100

    Δq = 130 - 80 = 50

    EI = (Δq/qmid) / (ΔI/Imid) = (50/105) / (100/150) = 0.476 / 0.667 = 0.714

    Since 0 < EI < 1, good X is a necessity.

    Note: Necessities have income elasticity between 0 and 1. The percentage change in quantity is less than the percentage change in income.

    Exercise 1.7: The demand function for good X is qX = 100 - 4P.

    a) Calculate the price elasticity at P = 10 and P = 20.
  • b) At what price is demand unit elastic?
  • c) What is the total revenue at the unit elastic price?
  • d) Draw the demand curve and indicate the elastic and inelastic regions.
    • Solution:

    Given: qX = 100 - 4P

    Total Revenue: TR = P × q = P(100 - 4P) = 100P - 4P2


    a) Price elasticities:

    E = (dq/dP) × (P/q) = (-4)(P/q)

    At P = 10: q = 100 - 4(10) = 60

    E = (-4)(10/60) = -40/60 = -0.667 (inelastic: |E| < 1)

    At P = 20: q = 100 - 4(20) = 20

    E = (-4)(20/20) = -80/20 = -4 (elastic: |E| > 1)

    b) Unit elastic price:

    Set |E| = 1:

    |(-4)(P/q)| = 1 → 4(P/q) = 1 → q = P/4

    Using demand function: 100 - 4P = P/4

    P/4 = 100/4 = 25

    At P = 25: demand is unit elastic.


    c) Total revenue at P = 25:

    q = 25, TR = 25 × 25 = 625

    Total revenue is maximized at the unit elastic point (this is a property of linear demand curves).


    d) Demand curve description:

    • For P < 25: E < -1, elastic region (quantity changes more than proportionally with price)
    • For P > 25: E > -1, elastic region (quantity changes more than proportionally with price)
    • The curve is a straight line passing through the point (25, 25).

    Exercise 1.8: Consider the following scenarios and classify each good as normal or inferior based on how demand responds to income changes:

    a) When a person's income increases by 10%, their demand for restaurant meals increases by 8%.
  • b) When income increases by 10%, demand for instant noodles decreases by 4%.
  • c) When income increases by 10%, demand for electricity increases by 6%.
  • d) When income increases by 10%, demand for bus tickets decreases by 3%.
  • e) When income increases by 10%, demand for branded clothing stays unchanged.
    • Solution:

    Income elasticity formula:

    EI = (%Δq/q) / (%ΔI/I)

    Since ΔI = 10%:

    Good %Δq EI Classification
    Restaurant meals +8% 8/10 = 0.8 Normal (luxury, since EI > 1)
    Instant noodles -4% -4/10 = -0.4 Inferior (since EI < 0)
    Electricity +6% 6/10 = 0.6 Normal (necessity, since 0 < E < 1)
    Bus tickets -3% -3/10 = -0.3 Inferior (since EI < 0)
    Branded clothing 0% 0/10 = 0 Unit inelastic (perfectly income-inelastic)

    e) Branded clothing is unit inelastic for income—demand doesn't change as income changes. This could indicate that for the relevant income range, consumers buy exactly one unit regardless of income level.

    Exercise 1.9: Given the utility function U(X, Y) = X0.3Y0.7, income I = 200, and prices PX = 4, PY = 5:

    a) Derive the demand functions for X and Y.
  • b) Calculate the cross-price elasticity of demand for good X with respect to good Y.
  • c) Calculate the income elasticity of demand for good X.
  • d) Calculate the income elasticity of demand for good Y.
    • Solution:

    For Cobb-Douglas utility U = XαYβ with α = 0.3, β = 0.7:


    a) Demand functions:

    X = (α/(α + β)) · (I/PX) = (0.3/1) × (200/4) = 0.3 × 50 = 15
    Y = (β/(α + β)) · (I/PY) = (0.7/1) × (200/5) = 0.7 × 40 = 28

    Optimal bundle: (15, 28)


    b) Cross-price elasticity EXY at the optimum:

    For Cobb-Douglas with α = 0.3, β = 0.7:

    qX = 0.3 × (I/PX)
    ∂qX/∂PX = -0.3I/PX2

    At the optimum (X = 15, Y = 28):

    EXY = [(-0.3)(200/42)] × (PY/qX)
    EXY = (-3) × (5/15) = -15/15 = -1

    Since EXY = -1 < 0, goods X and Y are complements. An increase in the price of Y reduces the quantity demanded of X.


    c) Income elasticity of demand for good X:

    EI = (α/(α + β)) = α = 0.3

    Since EI = 0.3 > 0, good X is a normal good with EI < 1 (luxury).


    d) Income elasticity of demand for good Y:

    EI = (β/(α + β)) = β = 0.7

    Since EI = 0.7 > 0, good Y is also a normal good, specifically a necessity since 0 < EI < 1.

    Exercise 1.10: Suppose the price of good X decreases from P1 to P2. A consumer's optimal consumption bundle changes from (X1, Y1) to (X2, Y2).

    a) Graphically decompose this change into substitution effect and income effect on an indifference curve diagram.
  • b) Calculate the substitution effect numerically.
  • c) Calculate the income effect numerically.
  • d) Verify that SE + IE = Total Effect.
    • Solution:

    Decomposition Framework:

    Total Effect (TE) = Substitution Effect (SE) + Income Effect (IE)
    X Low PX A B C SE IE
    Decomposition of Price Effect (Normal Good)

    b) Numerical Substitution Effect:

    SE is the change in consumption due to the change in relative prices, holding utility constant at the original level.

    To find SE:

    1. Find utility at original optimum: U1 = U(X1, Y1)
    2. At new price ratio, find the bundle on the same indifference curve
    3. SE = Change in X from original to this point

    c) Numerical Income Effect:

    IE is the change in consumption from the compensated bundle to the final bundle, reflecting the change in purchasing power.

    1. Find final bundle at new prices
    2. IE = Change in X from compensated bundle to final bundle

    d) Verification:

    TE = SE + IE

    The sum of substitution effect and income effect should equal the total change in quantity demanded.

    Exercise 1.11: Repeat Exercise 1.10 assuming good X is inferior.

    a) How would the decomposition differ?
  • b) Show the new diagram.
  • c) Calculate the numerical SE and IE values assuming the same magnitude of SE as before, but opposite direction of IE.
    • Solution:

    For an inferior good:

    • Substitution effect: Still moves toward the now cheaper good (same direction as before).
    • Income effect: Now moves in the opposite direction (negative IE).
    • Total effect: Depends on whether |IE| > |SE| or |SE| > |IE|.

    a) How the decomposition would differ:

    • With a normal good: SE and IE both increase X → TE = large increase.
    • With an inferior good: SE increases X, IE decreases X → TE depends on relative magnitudes.

    b) Diagram for inferior good:

    Decomposition for Inferior Good (IE opposes SE)

    c) Numerical SE and IE (assuming |IE| = |SE|):

    If the income effect is equal in magnitude but opposite in direction to the substitution effect:

    • If SE = +3 (X increases by 3), IE = -3 (X decreases by 3)
    • Then Total Effect = SE + IE = 0 (no net change in X)
    • This is called a unit income-elastic good (perfectly inelastic to income changes at the margin of utility level).

    Exercise 1.12: Using the Slutsky equation, verify the decomposition results from Exercises 1.10 and 1.11.

    For a consumer with U(X, Y) = XY:
    1. Calculate the Slutsky equation.
    2. Verify that EXX + EXY(PY/QX) + EXI(I/QX) = 0.
    3. Explain the economic meaning of each term.
    Solution:

    The Slutsky equation provides a decomposition of demand changes into substitution and income effects:

    EXX + EXY(PY/QX) + EXI(I/QX) = 0

    Term-by-term explanation:

    Term Formula Component Economic Meaning
    EXX (dX/dP)(P/Q) Arc price elasticity (own-price effect)
    EXY(PY/QX) Cross-price effect weighted by expenditure share Substitution effect (how X responds to change in PY)
    EXI(I/QX) Income effect weighted by expenditure share How X responds to change in income (real income)

    Verification:

    For U = XY, the expenditure share on X is 0.5 (since α = β for Cobb-Douglas with α + β = 1).

    At the optimal bundle: X = I/(2PX), so QX = I/(2PX)

    This means EXI(I/QX) = 1/2.

    The equation holds: EXX + EXY(PY/QX) + EXI = 0.5 + 0.5(PY/QX) + 0.5 = 0.5 + 0.5 = 1.

    Exercise 1.13: A market consists of two types of consumers. Type A consumers have demand qA = 30 - P, while Type B consumers have demand qB = 20 - 0.5P.

    a) Derive the market demand function.
  • b) Find the price elasticity of market demand.
  • c) If there are 100 Type A consumers and 200 Type B consumers, find the price elasticity of market demand at P = 10.
    • Solution:

    a) Market demand is the horizontal sum of individual demands:

    QD(P) = QA(P) + QB(P)
    QD(P) = (30 - P) + (20 - 0.5P) = 50 - 1.5P

    b) Price elasticity of market demand:

    ED = (dQD/dP) × (P/QD)

    dQD/dP = -1.5

    At P = 10: QD = 50 - 15 = 35

    ED = (-1.5)(10/35) = -15/35 = -0.429

    c) With 100 Type A and 200 Type B consumers:

    At P = 10:

    QA = 30 - 10 = 20 (each)
    QB = 20 - 5 = 15 (each)
    QD = 100(20) + 200(15) = 2000 + 3000 = 5000
    ED = (-1.5)(10/5000) = -15/5000 = -0.003

    The price elasticity is even more elastic when there are more Type B consumers (who are more elastic).

    Key Insight: Market elasticity depends on the distribution of elasticities across consumers. If one group is more elastic, it pulls the average toward higher elasticity.

    Key Concepts Review

    Core Concepts from Lecture 2:
    Concept Key Formula Important Property
    Individual Demand Derived from consumer optimization Shows how individual responds to prices
    Price Consumption Curve (PCC) Traces optimum as price varies Shape indicates normal/inferior
    Income Consumption Curve (ICC) Traces optimum as income varies Shape reflects good type
    Engel Curve Income vs. quantity relationship Slope = income elasticity
    Price Effect Decomposition PE = SE + IE SE always opposes price, IE depends on good type
    Normal Good EI > 0 IE reinforces SE
    Inferior Good EI < 0 IE opposes SE
    Giffen Good Strongly inferior |IE| > |SE|, violates law of demand
    Market Demand Horizontal sum of individual demands QD = ∑ qi
    Price Elasticity E = (%Δq)/(%ΔP) |E| > 1: elastic, |E| < 1: inelastic
    Cross-Price Elasticity EXY = (%ΔQX)/(%ΔPY) Positive: substitutes, Negative: complements
    Income Elasticity EI = (%Δq)/(%ΔI) Necessity: 0 < E < 1, Luxury: E > 1
    Exam Tips:
    1. Always identify whether you're dealing with normal or inferior goods when analyzing price effects.
    2. Remember that substitution effect always works in the opposite direction of the price change.
    3. For Giffen goods, both SE and IE are negative of the price decrease (but IE is larger).
    4. When summing individual demands, add quantities horizontally (same price).
    5. Use the Slutsky equation to decompose price effects into income and substitution components.
    6. Check your answers: SE + IE should equal the total change.

    Summary of Key Formulas

    Essential Formulas for Individual Demand and Elasticity:
    Formula What it means Application
    Cobb-Douglas Demand X = (α/(α+β))(I/PX) Constant expenditure share
    Cobb-Douglas Demand Y = (β/(α+β))(I/PY) Constant expenditure share
    Point Price Elasticity ED = (dq/dP)(P/q) Arc elasticity
    Arc Price Elasticity ED = (dq/dP)(P/q) Arc elasticity
    Unit Elastic E = -1 Maximum TR
    Total Revenue TR = P × q Price × quantity
    MR (Marginal Revenue) MR = dTR/dq Change in TR per unit
    Income Elasticity EI = (dq/dI)(I/q) Responsiveness to income
    Cross-Price Elasticity EXY = (dqX/dPY)(PY/qX) Substitutes: +, Complements: -

    Remember:
    • Individual demand is derived from utility maximization
    • PCC shows how optimal bundle responds to price changes
    • ICC shows how optimal bundle responds to income changes
    • Engel curve slope equals income elasticity
    • Price elasticity measures price responsiveness
    • Income elasticity measures income responsiveness
    • Cross-price elasticity measures related goods' effects
    • Normal goods: EI > 0, IE reinforces SE
    • Inferior goods: EI < 0, IE opposes SE
    • Giffen goods are rare theoretical cases
