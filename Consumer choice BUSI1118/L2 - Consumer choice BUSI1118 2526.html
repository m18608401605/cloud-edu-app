Individual Demand

Individual Demand refers to the quantity of a good that a single consumer is willing and able to purchase at various prices, holding other factors constant. It is derived from consumer choice theory by analyzing how a consumer's optimal consumption bundle changes as the price of one good varies.

Deriving Individual Demand from Indifference Curves

To derive the individual demand curve, we examine how the optimal quantity of a good changes as its price changes, while keeping income and the price of other goods constant.

Steps:

  1. Start with an initial price PX1 and find the consumer's optimal bundle (tangency point).
  2. Change the price of good X to PX2 and find the new optimal bundle.
  3. Repeat for various price levels.
  4. Plot the relationship between price and quantity demanded.

Price Consumption Curve (PCC)

Price Consumption Curve (PCC) - The locus of optimal consumption bundles as price of one good varies, holding income and price of other good constant.

The PCC traces how the optimal point moves as we rotate the budget line by changing the price of one good.

Income Consumption Curve (ICC)

Income Consumption Curve (ICC) - The locus of optimal consumption bundles as income varies, holding prices constant.

The ICC is derived by shifting the budget line outward (increase) or inward (decrease) parallel to itself. It shows how consumption of goods changes with income.

Deriving the ICC:

  1. Start with initial income I1 and find the optimal bundle.
  2. Increase income to I2 and find the new optimal bundle.
  3. Repeat for various income levels.
  4. Plot the relationship between income and optimal consumption.

ICC for Different Good Types:

Good Type ICC Direction Explanation
Normal Good X Positively sloped As income increases, X increases
Inferior Good X Negatively sloped As income increases, X decreases
Normal Good Y Positively sloped As income increases, Y increases

Engel Curve

Engel Curve - A curve showing the relationship between the quantity demanded of a good and the consumer's income, holding prices constant.

The Engel curve is derived from the Income Consumption Curve (ICC). It plots the quantity of a good on the horizontal axis against income on the vertical axis.

Types of Engel Curves

1. Engel Curve for Normal Goods

Normal Good - A good for which demand increases when income increases.

The Engel curve for a normal good has a positive slope (as income increases, quantity demanded increases).

2. Engel Curve for Inferior Goods

Inferior Good - A good for which demand decreases when income increases.

The Engel curve for an inferior good has a negative slope (as income increases, quantity demanded decreases).

Substitution and Income Effects - Detailed Analysis

Decomposing the Price Effect

Price Effect Decomposition:
Total Effect = Substitution Effect + Income Effect

Substitution Effect (SE)

The substitution effect is the change in consumption due to the change in relative prices, holding utility constant. The consumer substitutes toward the now relatively cheaper good.

Key Properties of SE:
  1. Always moves in the opposite direction of the price change
  2. If PX falls: SE increases consumption of X
  3. If PX rises: SE decreases consumption of X
  4. Moves along the same indifference curve (constant utility)

Income Effect (IE)

The income effect is the change in consumption due to the change in purchasing power. A price decrease effectively increases real income (consumer becomes "richer"), while a price increase effectively decreases real income (consumer becomes "poorer").

Direction of Income Effect:
Good Type IE Direction Relationship to SE
Normal Good Positive (buy more when richer) Reinforces SE
Inferior Good Negative (buy less when richer) Opposes SE

Graphical Decomposition of Price Effect

Compensated Demand - The relationship between price and quantity demanded holding utility constant. This isolates the substitution effect.
Uncompensated Demand - The relationship between price and quantity demanded holding income constant. This includes both substitution and income effects.

The compensated budget line is the budget line that would give the consumer the same utility at the new prices, used to find the substitution effect.

Decomposition of Price Effect: A→B is SE, B→C is IE
Understanding the Decomposition:
  • A to B (Substitution Effect): Movement along the same indifference curve from original price to new price ratio. This isolates the pure substitution response to the price change.
  • B to C (Income Effect): Movement from the compensated budget line to the final budget line. This captures the change in consumption due to the change in purchasing power.
  • A to C (Total Effect): The sum of SE + IE, representing the complete response to the price change.

Normal vs. Inferior Goods - Detailed Analysis

Normal Goods

Normal Good - A good for which demand increases when income increases (and decreases when income decreases).
Characteristic Description
Income Elasticity EI > 0 (positive)
Income Effect Direction Same as substitution effect (reinforces)
Price Decrease SE increases X, IE increases X → Total = increase in X
Price Increase SE decreases X, IE decreases X → Total = decrease in X
Examples of Normal Goods:
  • Restaurant meals (as opposed to home cooking)
  • New cars (as opposed to used cars)
  • Electronics and appliances
  • Brand-name clothing (as opposed to second-hand)
  • Travel and vacations
Page 8

Inferior Goods

Inferior Good - A good for which demand decreases when income increases (and increases when income decreases).
Characteristic Description
Income Elasticity EI < 0 (negative)
Income Effect Direction Opposite to substitution effect
Price Decrease SE increases X, IE decreases X → Total depends on magnitudes
Price Increase SE decreases X, IE increases X → Total depends on magnitudes
Examples of Inferior Goods:
  • Instant noodles and other low-cost food items
  • Used clothing and second-hand items
  • Public transportation (as opposed to owning a car)
  • Rental housing (as opposed to owned housing)
  • Generic brands (as opposed to premium brands)
Economic Rationale: Inferior goods are typically lower-quality substitutes for normal goods. When consumers' income increases, they can "trade up" to higher-quality alternatives, reducing demand for the inferior good.

Giffen Goods - Special Case

Giffen Good - A special type of inferior good where the negative income effect is so strong that it outweighs the substitution effect, resulting in a positive relationship between price and quantity demanded (violating the law of demand).

Conditions for Giffen Goods

Required Conditions:
  1. The good must be inferior (EI < 0)
  2. The good must take a large portion of the consumer's budget
  3. There must be few or no close substitutes
  4. Income effect must be strong enough to overcome substitution effect: |IE| > |SE|

The Giffen Paradox

Historical Example - Irish Potato Famine (1845):

Sir Robert Giffen observed that as potato prices rose during the famine, Irish peasants (who were very poor and consumed mostly potatoes) actually bought more potatoes, not fewer.

Explanation:

  1. Potatoes were an inferior good for the poor
  2. Potatoes constituted a large portion of their budget
  3. There were few substitutes for potatoes (staple food)
  4. When potato prices rose, their real income fell dramatically (they became poorer)
  5. The income effect (buying more potatoes to survive) outweighed the substitution effect (buying fewer potatoes)
Important Note: Giffen goods are primarily theoretical. They have been observed in rare historical cases but are not commonly found in modern, diversified economies. Most goods that economists study obey the law of demand.

Market Demand

Market Demand - The total quantity of a good that all consumers in a market are willing and able to purchase at various prices.

Deriving Market Demand

Market demand is obtained by horizontally summing individual demand curves. This means we add up the quantities demanded by each consumer at each price level.

QD(P) = q1(P) + q2(P) + ... + qn(P)

where qi(P) is the quantity demanded by consumer i at price P, and there are n consumers in the market.

Graphical Derivation

Deriving Market Demand by Horizontal Summation
Key Point: Market demand curve is typically flatter than individual demand curves because there are more consumers in the market. A price decrease leads to a larger increase in total market quantity compared to any individual consumer's response.

Example: Deriving Market Demand

Numerical Example:

Consider three consumers with the following demand functions for good X:

  • Consumer 1: q1(P) = 30 - 2P
  • Consumer 2: q2(P) = 20 - P
  • Consumer 3: q3(P) = 10 - P
Market Demand at Different Prices:
Price (P) Consumer 1 Consumer 2 Consumer 3 Market Demand
$5 30 - 2(5) = 20 20 - 5 = 15 10 - 5 = 5 20 + 15 + 5 = 40
$10 30 - 2(10) = 10 20 - 10 = 10 10 - 10 = 0 10 + 10 + 0 = 20
$15 30 - 2(15) = 0 20 - 15 = 5 10 - 15 = -5 → 0 0 + 5 + 0 = 5

Market Demand Function: QD(P) = 40 - 4P for P ≤ $10, and QD(P) = 15 - P for P > $10 (because consumer 3 stops buying at P = $15).

Price Elasticity of Demand

Price Elasticity of Demand (ED) - A measure of responsiveness of quantity demanded to a change in price.

Formula

ED = (% Change in Quantity Demanded) / (% Change in Price)
ED = (ΔQ/Q) / (ΔP/P) = (ΔQ/ΔP) × (P/Q)

where:

  • ΔQ = Change in quantity demanded
  • ΔP = Change in price
  • Q = Initial quantity demanded
  • P = Initial price

Types of Price Elasticity

Type Elasticity Value Description Impact of Price Change
Perfectly Inelastic ED = 0 Quantity demanded does not change No effect on TR
Inelastic 0 < |ED| < 1 %ΔQ < |%ΔP| TR increases when P increases
Unit Elastic |ED| = 1 %ΔQ = %ΔP TR at maximum
Elastic |ED| > 1 %ΔQ > |%ΔP| TR decreases when P increases
Perfectly Elastic ED = ∞ Infinite response to price change P×Q → 0 as P → 0
Understanding Total Revenue (TR):
  • When |E| > 1 (elastic): Price increase → TR decreases (quantity falls more than price rises)
  • When |E| < 1 (inelastic): Price increase → TR increases (quantity falls less than price rises)
  • When |E| = 1 (unit elastic): TR is at maximum (doesn't change with small price changes)

Other Elasticities

Income Elasticity of Demand

Income Elasticity of Demand (EI) - Measures the responsiveness of quantity demanded to changes in income.
EI = (% Change in Quantity Demanded) / (% Change in Income)
EI = (ΔQ/Q) / (ΔI/I)
Type of Good EI Value Classification
Normal Good EI > 0 Positive relationship with income
Inferior Good EI < 0 Negative relationship with income
Necessity 0 < EI < 1 Quantity increases less than proportionally
Luxury EI > 1 Quantity increases more than proportionally

Cross-Price Elasticity of Demand

Cross-Price Elasticity of Demand (EXY) - Measures the responsiveness of quantity demanded of good X to changes in the price of good Y.
EXY = (% Change in QX) / (% Change in PY)
Relationship EXY Value Classification
Substitutes EXY > 0 (positive) An increase in PY increases demand for X
Complements EXY < 0 (negative) An increase in PY decreases demand for X
Unrelated EXY = 0 Price of Y has no effect on demand for X

Relationship Between Elasticities

Key Relationships:
Concept Formula
Marshallian (Uncompensated) ED ED = (ΔQ/ΔP)(P/Q)
Hicksian (Compensated) ED ED = (ΔQ/ΔP) at constant U
Slutsky Equation EXX + EXY(PY/QX) + EXI(I/QX) = 0
Slutsky Equation Explained:
  • EXX: Own-price elasticity of demand (how much quantity X responds to its own price)
  • EXY(PY/QX): Cross-price elasticity weighted by expenditure share (substitution effect)
  • EXI(I/QX): Income elasticity weighted by expenditure share (income effect)
  • Sum = 0: The equation states that the weighted sum equals zero

Key Takeaways

Core Concepts from Consumer Choice to Market Demand:
  1. Individual demand is derived by varying price and tracking optimal bundle
  2. PCC shows how consumption responds to price changes
  3. ICC shows how consumption responds to income changes
  4. Engel curve plots income-quantity relationship
  5. Market demand is horizontal sum of individual demands
  6. Price elasticity measures price responsiveness
  7. Income elasticity measures income responsiveness
  8. Cross-price elasticity measures related goods' price effects
  9. Giffen goods violate law of demand (theoretical rarity)
  10. Slutsky equation decomposes demand into income and substitution effects
Practical Applications:
  • Firms use elasticity estimates to predict revenue changes from price changes
  • Governments use elasticity to predict tax incidence
  • Businesses use income elasticity to forecast demand as economy grows
  • Cross-price elasticity used in merger analysis and antitrust cases
