Section B Style Question 1

Practice Question · NPV Full Calculation · 20 marks

🔢 NPV + Annuity + Decision

Riverside plc is considering investing in a new machine. The following data are available:

ItemAmount
Cost of machine (Year 0)£80,000
Annual net cash inflow (Years 1–5)£22,000
Scrap value at end of Year 5£5,000
Cost of capital12% p.a.

Required: Calculate the NPV and advise whether to proceed. (20 marks)

✅ Full Solution

AF(12%, 5 years) = 3.605 | DF(12%, Year 5) = 0.567

PV of annual cash flows: £22,000 × 3.605£79,310
PV of scrap value: £5,000 × 0.567£2,835
Total PV of inflows£82,145
Less: Initial investment−£80,000
NPV+£2,145

Decision: NPV is positive (+£2,145), so Riverside plc should PROCEED. The project creates £2,145 of shareholder wealth. Note: the scrap value is a single cash flow in Year 5, so use the discount factor (not annuity factor) for that component.

Section B Style Question 2

Practice Question · IRR Interpolation · 15 marks

🔢 IRR Interpolation + NPV Comparison

Using the same Riverside plc data above, you have calculated:

Discount RateNPV
12%+£2,145
15%−£3,820

Required: (a) Calculate the approximate IRR. (b) Should the project be accepted if the cost of capital is 13%? (15 marks)

✅ Full Solution

(a) IRR by Interpolation:

IRR ≈ 12% + [2,145 ÷ (2,145 + 3,820)] × (15% − 12%)
= 12% + [2,145 ÷ 5,965] × 3%
= 12% + 0.3597 × 3%
IRR ≈13.08%

(b) Decision at 13% cost of capital:

IRR (13.08%) > Cost of Capital (13%) → ACCEPT (marginally). However, the margin is very small — in practice, management should consider the sensitivity of this result to estimation errors in cash flows.

Problem 4.1 — Barclay plc

McLaney Business Finance 11th Ed · Ch.4 · NPV & Discounted Payback Period

🔢 NPV + Discounted PBP + Decision Conflict

Barclay plc is considering a project with the following cash flows:

YearCash Flow
Year 0 (Investment)−£10m
Year 1+£5m
Year 2+£4m
Year 3+£3m
Year 4+£2m

Cost of finance: 15%. Maximum acceptable discounted payback period: 3 years.

Required: Should the project be undertaken on the basis of (a) NPV and (b) discounted payback period?

✅ Full Solution

(a) NPV Calculation

Year 1: £5m ÷ 1.15£4.348m
Year 2: £4m ÷ 1.15²£3.025m
Year 3: £3m ÷ 1.15³£1.975m
Year 4: £2m ÷ 1.15⁴£1.144m
Total PV of inflows£10.492m
Less: Initial investment−£10.000m
NPV+£0.492m → ACCEPT

(b) Discounted Payback Period

Cumulative PV after Year 1£4.348m
Cumulative PV after Year 2£7.373m
Cumulative PV after Year 3£9.348m (still < £10m)
Cumulative PV after Year 4£10.492m (exceeds £10m)

Discounted PBP falls between Year 3 and Year 4 — exceeds the 3-year maximum → REJECT on PBP criterion.

Conflict: NPV says ACCEPT; discounted PBP says REJECT. NPV is the theoretically correct method — it directly measures shareholder wealth creation. PBP ignores cash flows beyond the payback date (here, the Year 4 inflow of £1.144m PV). The project should be accepted on NPV grounds.

Problem 4.2 — Branton & Co

McLaney Business Finance 11th Ed · Ch.4 · Mutually Exclusive Projects · NPV, IRR & Payback

🔢 NPV + IRR Interpolation + Payback · Mutually Exclusive

Branton & Co is choosing between two mutually exclusive projects. Cost of finance: 10%.

YearProject AProject B
Year 0 (Investment)−£50,000−£36,000
Year 1+£39,000+£28,000
Year 2+£9,000+£8,000
Year 3 (operating)+£12,000+£14,000
Year 3 (residual value)+£7,000+£6,000

Required: (a) NPV for both projects. (b) Approximate IRR for Project A. (c) Payback period for both.

✅ Full Solution

(a) NPV Calculation

Discount factors at 10%: Y1 = 0.909, Y2 = 0.826, Y3 = 0.751

Project A: 39/1.1 + 9/1.21 + 19/1.331 − 50
= 35.45 + 7.44 + 14.27 − 50NPV_A = +£7,160
Project B: 28/1.1 + 8/1.21 + 20/1.331 − 36
= 25.45 + 6.61 + 15.03 − 36NPV_B = +£11,090

Decision: Choose Project B — higher NPV means greater shareholder wealth creation.

(b) Approximate IRR for Project A (by interpolation)

At 20%: NPV_A ≈ +£1,300
At 25%: NPV_A ≈ −£2,100
IRR ≈ 20% + [1,300 ÷ (1,300 + 2,100)] × (25% − 20%)
= 20% + (1,300 ÷ 3,400) × 5%IRR_A ≈ 21.9%

(c) Payback Period

Project A: £50k − £39k = £11k remaining after Y1; £11k − £9k = £2k after Y2
PBP_A = 2 years + (2/12) months ≈ 2 years 2 months
Project B: £36k − £28k = £8k remaining after Y1; £8k − £8k = £0 after Y2
PBP_B = exactly 2 years

On payback, Project B recovers investment faster. All three methods favour Project B. Note: NPV is the primary decision criterion — it correctly accounts for time value and total wealth creation.

Ch.4 Problem 4.2 — Branton & Co (Mutually Exclusive Projects)

Textbook Problem · NPV + IRR + Payback · McLaney 11th Ed Ch.4

🔢 Mutually Exclusive · NPV vs IRR Conflict
YearProject A (£000)Project B (£000)
0−50−36
1+39+28
2+9+8
3 (incl. residual)+12 + £7k residual+14 + £6k residual

Cost of finance: 10%.

Required: (a) NPV for both projects. (b) Approximate IRR for Project A. (c) Payback period for both. Which project should be chosen?

✅ Solution

(a) NPV @ 10%:

NPV_A = 39/1.1 + 9/1.21 + 19/1.331 − 50 = 35.45 + 7.44 + 14.27 − 50 = +£7.16k

NPV_B = 28/1.1 + 8/1.21 + 20/1.331 − 36 = 25.45 + 6.61 + 15.03 − 36 = +£11.09k

(b) IRR for Project A (interpolation):

At 20%: NPV ≈ +£1.3k. At 25%: NPV ≈ −£2.1k

IRR ≈ 20% + [1.3/(1.3+2.1)] × 5% ≈ 21.9%

(c) Payback:

Project A: 50 − 39 = 11 remaining after Y1. Y2 inflow = 9, still short by 2. PBP ≈ 2 + 2/12 = 2.2 years

Project B: 36 − 28 = 8 remaining after Y1. Y2 inflow = 8. PBP = 2.0 years

Decision: Choose Project B — higher NPV (+£11.09k vs +£7.16k). When projects are mutually exclusive, always use NPV, not IRR or payback.
