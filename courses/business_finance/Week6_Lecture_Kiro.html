📈 Part 1 — Ordinary Shares 普通股

1.1 Characteristics 特征

费曼类比 Feynman Analogy

把公司融资想象成开餐厅借钱：银行贷款（债务）是你向朋友借的钱，每月固定还利息，朋友不管你生意好不好都要收利息，但生意倒了他先拿回钱。普通股股东像是合伙人——生意好了分红，生意差了不分，倒闭了最后才能拿剩余资产。风险最高，所以要求回报也最高。

Ordinary shareholders = residual claimants. Highest risk → highest required return. No fixed obligation for the company.

Ordinary shareholders are residual claimants — they get paid last, after all creditors and preference shareholders. In return they receive:

  • Voting rights — one share, one vote (typically)
  • Dividends — not guaranteed; paid only if the board declares them
  • Capital gains — if the share price rises

Advantages for the Company

  • No fixed obligation — dividends can be cut or omitted in hard times
  • Permanent capital — no repayment date, strengthens the balance sheet
  • Improves debt capacity (lower gearing)

Disadvantages for the Company

  • Most expensive source of finance — shareholders bear the highest risk, so they demand the highest return
  • Dilutes control — new shareholders get voting rights
  • Dividends paid from after-tax profits (no tax shield)

1.2 Rights Issue 配股

A rights issue offers new shares to existing shareholders at a discount to the current market price, in proportion to their existing holdings. This protects existing shareholders from dilution.

Theoretical Ex-Rights Price (TERP)
TERP = (N × Pold + Pissue) / (N + 1)
N = number of existing shares needed to get 1 new share  |  P_old = current market price  |  P_issue = issue price
📝 Worked Example

4-for-1 Rights Issue

A company offers a 4-for-1 rights issue at £2.50. Current share price = £3.20.

ItemValue
Existing shares (N)4
Current price (P_old)£3.20
Issue price (P_issue)£2.50
TERP = (4 × 3.20 + 2.50) / 5£3.06

After the rights issue, the share price should theoretically fall to £3.06. Shareholders are no better or worse off — the value of their rights compensates for the price drop.

🆕 Part 2 — Preference Shares 优先股

2.1 Key Features

  • Fixed dividend (expressed as % of nominal value) — paid before ordinary dividends
  • Cumulative preference shares: unpaid dividends accumulate and must be paid before ordinary dividends resume
  • Non-cumulative: missed dividends are lost forever
  • No voting rights (usually) — unless dividends are in arrears
⚠️ Exam Pitfall

Preference dividends are NOT tax-deductible — they are paid from after-tax profits, just like ordinary dividends. This makes preference shares more expensive than debt despite having a fixed payment.

Tax shield applies to DEBT interest only — not preference dividends!

💵 Part 3 — Bonds & Loan Notes debt;伎与贷款票据

3.1 Bond Basics

A bond (or loan note / debenture) is a long-term debt instrument with:

  • Face value (par value) — typically £100, repaid at maturity
  • Coupon rate — fixed annual interest as % of face value
  • Maturity date — when the principal is repaid

Secured vs Unsecured

  • Secured debentures: backed by specific assets (fixed charge) or all assets (floating charge) — lower risk to lender, lower interest rate
  • Unsecured loan notes: no asset backing — higher risk, higher coupon required

3.2 Tax Shield on Debt

费曼类比 Feynman Analogy

把税盾想象成政府替你付了一部分利息：你借钱付8%利息，但这8%可以从利润里扣掉再交税。税率30%，相当于政府帮你承担了 8%×30%=2.4% 的利息，你实际只付 5.6%。股权股息没有这个优惠——股息是税后利润分配，政府不补贴。这就是为什么债务比股权便宜。

Interest is tax-deductible → government subsidises part of the cost. Dividends are not → no subsidy. This is the core reason debt is cheaper than equity.

Interest payments on debt are tax-deductible. This reduces the effective cost of debt to the company — the government effectively subsidises part of the interest cost.

After-Tax Cost of Debt
Kd(after-tax) = Coupon Rate × (1 − Tax Rate)
Also written as: Kd × (1 − t)
📝 Worked Example

8% Bond, 30% Corporation Tax

ItemValue
Coupon rate8%
Tax rate (t)30%
After-tax cost = 8% × (1 − 0.30)5.6%

The company pays 8% interest but the effective cost is only 5.6% because the 2.4% tax saving reduces the net outflow.

💡 Why Debt is Cheaper than Equity

1. Lower risk to the lender — debt holders are paid before shareholders, so they accept a lower return.

2. Tax deductibility — interest reduces taxable profit, creating a government subsidy. Dividends do not.

These two factors together make debt the cheapest source of long-term finance (though too much debt increases financial risk).

🔄 Part 4 — Convertible Bonds 可转换debt;伎

4.1 How Convertibles Work

费曼类比 Feynman Analogy

可转换债券就像买了一张"期权票"的债券：你先借给公司钱（收低利息），同时拿到一张票，说"如果公司股价涨到某个价格，我可以把债券换成股票"。公司股价涨了你换股票赚差价；股价没涨你就继续收利息拿回本金。对投资者：下有保底（债券），上有弹性（股票）。对公司：初始利率低，且如果转换了就不用还本金。

Convertible = bond + call option on shares. Lower coupon for company; upside potential for investor. Win-win — until conversion dilutes existing shareholders.

A convertible bond starts as debt but gives the holder the option to convert into ordinary shares at a predetermined price/ratio.

  • Conversion ratio: number of shares received per bond
  • Conversion price: effective price paid per share on conversion
  • Conversion premium: how much above the current share price the conversion price is
Conversion Price
Conversion Price = Bond Face Value / Conversion Ratio
e.g. £100 bond converting to 40 shares → Conversion Price = £100/40 = £2.50
Conversion Premium
Premium = (Conversion Price − Current Share Price) / Current Share Price × 100%
Measures how far the share price must rise before conversion is worthwhile
📝 Worked Example

Convertible Bond Analysis

A £100 bond converts into 40 shares. Current share price = £2.20.

CalculationResult
Conversion price = £100 / 40£2.50
Conversion premium = (2.50 − 2.20) / 2.20 × 100%13.6%

The share price must rise by at least 13.6% before conversion becomes financially attractive to the bondholder.

4.2 Why Companies Issue Convertibles

  • Lower coupon rate — investors accept less interest in exchange for the conversion option
  • Deferred equity — company avoids immediate dilution; shares only issued if conversion occurs
  • No immediate dilution of EPS or control at issue date
  • Attractive to investors who want downside protection (bond floor) with upside potential (equity)
🏠 Part 5 — Leasing 租赁

5.1 Types of Lease

Operating Lease

  • Short-term — lease period is less than the asset's useful life
  • Lessor bears obsolescence risk — they own the asset and must re-lease or sell it
  • Under old rules: off-balance-sheet (kept debt ratios artificially low)

Finance Lease

  • Long-term — covers most of the asset's useful life
  • Lessee bears risks and rewards of ownership (even though legal title stays with lessor)
  • On-balance-sheet — asset and liability both recognised

Sale and Leaseback

Company sells an asset (e.g. property) to a financial institution, then leases it back immediately. This raises cash while retaining use of the asset.

5.2 Advantages of Leasing

  • Preserves cash — no large upfront capital outlay
  • Avoids obsolescence — especially with operating leases on technology
  • 100% financing — unlike bank loans which may require a deposit
  • Lease payments may be tax-deductible
⚠️ IFRS 16 Pitfall

Under IFRS 16 (effective 2019), the distinction between operating and finance leases has largely been eliminated for lessees. Almost all leases must now be recognised on the balance sheet as a right-of-use asset and a lease liability.

The "off-balance-sheet" advantage of operating leases is gone under IFRS 16!
★ Part 6 — Islamic Finance 伊斯兰金融

6.1 Core Principle: Prohibition of Riba

Islamic finance is governed by Sharia law, which prohibits riba (interest). The principle is that money cannot earn money simply by existing — returns must be linked to real economic activity and risk-sharing.

6.2 Key Instruments

InstrumentConventional EquivalentHow It Works
MurabahaLoan / trade financeBank buys the asset and sells it to the customer at a cost-plus markup, payable in instalments. No interest — the profit is built into the price.
IjaraLeasingBank buys and owns the asset, then leases it to the customer for a fee. Similar to a finance lease but structured to avoid interest.
MusharakaEquity / partnershipProfit-sharing partnership — bank and customer jointly own the asset/business and share profits and losses according to agreed ratios.
SukukBondsIslamic bonds — asset-backed securities where investors receive a share of profits from the underlying asset, not interest payments.
💡 Key Insight: Same Economic Effect, Different Structure

Islamic finance instruments achieve the same economic outcomes as conventional finance — funding asset purchases, raising capital, providing liquidity — but are structured around profit-sharing, asset ownership, and trade rather than interest-bearing loans.

For example, a Murabaha transaction and a conventional loan both allow a business to acquire an asset and pay over time. The difference is that in Murabaha, the bank takes temporary ownership of the asset and earns a trade profit, not interest.
