Question 1 of 10
Pearson Ch.11 MCQ
A profitable business is partly financed by loan notes. If there were to be an increase in the rate of Corporation Tax, the value of the business would:
A: increase.
B: decrease.
C: stay the same.
D: any one of the above depending on the business's level of gearing and its operating cost structure.
✅ Answer: A — increase

Under MM with taxes: VL = VU + t × D

If the corporation tax rate t increases, the tax shield value (t × D) increases, so VL increases.

Intuition: A higher tax rate means the government "subsidises" more of the interest cost. The debt tax shield becomes more valuable, boosting firm value.

⚠️ Pitfall: Don't confuse the effect on the firm's tax bill (which rises) with the effect on firm value (which also rises, because the tax shield is worth more).
Question 2 of 10
Pearson Ch.11 MCQ
The beta of a business with some financial (capital) gearing is:
A: the simple (unweighted) average of the betas of the equity and loan finance.
B: the beta of the business's equity plus the beta of the business's loan finance.
C: the beta of the business's equity less the beta of the business's loan finance.
D: the average of the betas of the equity and loan finance, weighted by their market values.
✅ Answer: D — weighted average by market values

The asset (business) beta reflects the overall risk of the firm's assets. It equals the market-value-weighted average of equity beta and debt beta:

βasset = (VE/VL) × βequity + (VD/VL) × βdebt

Since debt is relatively safe, βdebt ≈ 0, so in practice:
βasset ≈ (VE/VL) × βequity

This means βequity > βasset for a geared firm — equity holders bear more risk than the underlying business.
Question 3 of 10
Pearson Ch.11 MCQ
In Modigliani and Miller's original (no taxes) assertion on capital gearing, they show that increasing the proportion of debt:
A: decreases the weighted average cost of capital.
B: increases the value of the business.
C: increases the cost of equity.
D: increases the value of each of its equity shares.
✅ Answer: C — increases the cost of equity

Under MM (no tax): VL = VU and WACC is constant. So how can adding cheap debt keep WACC unchanged?

Answer: As debt increases, equity holders face more financial risk (they're last in line if the firm fails). They demand a higher return to compensate → Ke rises.

The rise in Ke exactly offsets the benefit of cheaper debt, keeping WACC constant and firm value unchanged.

⚠️ Pitfall: Options A and B describe MM with taxes. In the no-tax world, neither WACC falls nor firm value rises.
Question 4 of 10
Pearson Ch.11 MCQ
In Modigliani and Miller's revised (with taxes) assertion on capital gearing, which one of the following will NOT result from increasing the proportion of debt?
A: An increase in the value of the business.
B: An increase in the value of each of its equity shares.
C: A decrease in the weighted average cost of capital.
D: A decrease in the cost of debt.
✅ Answer: D — A decrease in the cost of debt

Under MM with taxes, increasing debt:
• A ✓ — VL = VU + tD increases
• B ✓ — Higher firm value → higher equity value per share
• C ✓ — WACC falls as more cheap (tax-shielded) debt is used
• D ✗ — The cost of debt Kd is set by the market (lenders' required return). It is not affected by how much debt the firm issues — it depends on the firm's credit risk.

⚠️ Pitfall: This is a "NOT" question. The cost of debt is determined by the market, not by the firm's capital structure decisions.
Question 5 of 10
Pearson Ch.11 MCQ
The traditional view of capital gearing is that:
A: there is no optimal capital structure.
B: a business with zero debt can increase its value by substituting some debt for equity in its capital structure.
C: a business with zero debt will increase its business risk by substituting some debt for equity in its capital structure.
D: a business with zero debt can increase its weighted average cost of capital by substituting some debt for equity in its capital structure.
✅ Answer: B — adding some debt increases value

The traditional view holds that an all-equity firm can increase its value by introducing some debt, because:
• Debt is cheaper than equity (lower required return)
• Interest is tax-deductible (tax shield benefit)
• At low gearing levels, financial distress risk is minimal

However, beyond the optimal point, financial distress costs outweigh the tax shield benefit, so value falls.

A is wrong — the traditional view explicitly says an optimal structure exists.
D is wrong — adding cheap debt initially lowers WACC, not raises it.
Question 6 of 10
Pearson Ch.11 MCQ
When deducing the 'adjusted' net present value (APV) of a project, it is necessary to:
A: calculate the NPV as if there were no financial risk and adjust it by an amount to allow for financial risk.
B: deduce the NPV as if the project were undertaken by an ungeared business and make an adjustment for the tax benefit arising from the fact that the project is being undertaken by a geared business.
C: adjust the cash flow estimations to allow for financial risk.
D: adjust the discount rate to allow for the additional risk to which gearing gives rise.
✅ Answer: B — APV = ungeared NPV + PV(tax shield)

APV separates the investment decision from the financing decision:

APV = Base-case NPV + PV(financing side effects)

• Base-case NPV: discount project cash flows at the ungeared cost of equity (as if all-equity financed)
• Financing side effects: mainly the PV of the debt tax shield

This is particularly useful when the capital structure changes over the project's life (e.g., LBOs, project finance).

⚠️ Pitfall: APV does NOT adjust the discount rate (that's WACC approach). It keeps the discount rate clean and adds financing benefits separately.
Question 7 of 10
Pearson Ch.11 MCQ
According to Modigliani and Miller, in their 'with taxes' analysis, the relationship of the value of a geared business (VG), the value of its ungeared counterpart (VU), the Corporation Tax rate (T) and the value of the debt (D) is:
A: VG = VU + TD
B: VU = D + TVG
C: VU = VG + TD
D: VG = D + TVU
✅ Answer: A — VG = VU + TD

This is the core MM with-tax formula. The geared firm is worth more than the ungeared firm by exactly the present value of the perpetual tax shield:

PV(tax shield) = T × Kd × D / Kd = T × D

(Assuming debt is permanent and the tax shield is discounted at Kd)

Memory trick: VG > VU because gearing adds value via tax shield. So VG = VU + something positive (TD). Options C and D have the inequality backwards.
Question 8 of 10
Pearson Ch.11 MCQ
A business which has high 'operating gearing' has:
A: high interest charges compared to its pre-interest profit.
B: a high proportion of fixed costs in its total costs figure.
C: a high proportion of variable costs in its total cost figure.
D: a large proportion of debt in its capital structure.
✅ Answer: B — high proportion of fixed costs

Operating gearing relates to the cost structure of the business (fixed vs variable costs), NOT to debt financing.

• High fixed costs → profits are highly sensitive to revenue changes → high operating risk
• Example: Airlines (high fixed costs: planes, staff) vs. consultancies (mostly variable costs)

⚠️ Pitfall: Don't confuse operating gearing with financial gearing:
• Operating gearing = fixed costs / total costs (cost structure)
• Financial gearing = debt / total capital (capital structure)
Options A and D describe financial gearing, not operating gearing.
Question 9 of 10
Pearson Ch.11 MCQ
The existence of potential bankruptcy costs, when coupled with Modigliani and Miller's (with taxes) assertion on capital gearing, tends to mean that:
A: capital gearing has little or no effect on the business's weighted average cost of capital.
B: businesses seek to gear up to very high levels.
C: there is likely to be an optimum capital gearing level.
D: businesses regard capital gearing as of no net benefit.
✅ Answer: C — there is likely to be an optimum capital gearing level

MM with taxes alone says "gear up as much as possible" (more debt = more tax shield = more value). But add bankruptcy costs:

VL = VU + PV(tax shield) − PV(bankruptcy costs)

At low gearing: tax shield benefit > bankruptcy cost → value rises with debt
At high gearing: bankruptcy cost rises sharply → value starts to fall

The optimal point is where marginal tax shield benefit = marginal bankruptcy cost. This is the Trade-off Theory of capital structure.

B is wrong — bankruptcy costs prevent firms from gearing up indefinitely.
Question 10 of 10
Pearson Ch.11 MCQ
A business is all-equity financed and has a cost of equity of 25% a year. The directors intend to expand the business by issuing debentures with an interest cost of 10% a year, to the value of 40% of the existing value of the business. Making the Modigliani and Miller assumptions, including that of no taxes, what will be the cost of equity after the debenture issue?
A: 29.3%
B: 35.0%
C: 31.0%
D: 29.0%
✅ Answer: B — 35.0%

Under MM (no tax), WACC is constant = K0 = 25% (the all-equity cost).

After issuing debt worth 40% of original firm value:
• D = 0.40 × VU, so E = 0.60 × VU
• D/E = 0.40/0.60 = 2/3

Using MM no-tax Ke formula:
Ke = K0 + (K0 − Kd) × D/E
Ke = 25% + (25% − 10%) × (2/3)
Ke = 25% + 15% × 0.667
Ke = 25% + 10% = 35%

⚠️ Pitfall: The D/E ratio uses the new capital structure values. D = 40% of original V, E = remaining 60% of original V (since VL = VU under no-tax MM). So D/E = 40/60 = 2/3, not 40/100.

Question B1 — MM With Tax: Firm Value & WACC

📋 Scenario: Orion plc

Orion plc is currently all-equity financed. Its equity has a market value of £400 million and its cost of equity is 18%. The company is considering issuing £150 million of permanent debt at an interest rate of 8%. The proceeds will be used to repurchase shares. The corporation tax rate is 30%.

(a) Calculate the value of Orion plc after the debt issue (MM with tax).
(b) Calculate the new WACC after the debt issue.
(c) Explain why the WACC falls when debt is introduced (under MM with tax).

📖 Click to reveal full worked solution

Part (a): New Firm Value

V_U = £400m (current all-equity value)
PV(tax shield) = t × D = 0.30 × £150m = £45m
V_L = V_U + tD = £400m + £45m = £445m

Part (b): New WACC

After the debt issue:

Equity value = V_L − D = £445m − £150m = £295m
We = 295/445 = 66.3% | Wd = 150/445 = 33.7%

New cost of equity (MM with tax):

Ke_new = Ke_U + (Ke_U − Kd) × (D/E) × (1−t)
= 18% + (18% − 8%) × (150/295) × 0.70
= 18% + 10% × 0.508 × 0.70 = 18% + 3.56% = 21.56%
WACC = 0.663 × 21.56% + 0.337 × 8% × (1−0.30)
= 14.29% + 1.89% = 16.18%

Alternatively: WACC = Ke_U × (1 − t×D/V_L) = 18% × (1 − 0.30×150/445) = 18% × 0.899 = 16.2%

Part (c): Why WACC Falls

Under MM with tax, introducing debt lowers WACC because:

  • Debt is cheaper than equity (lenders bear less risk)
  • Interest is tax-deductible — the government effectively subsidises part of the debt cost
  • Although Ke rises (equity holders face more financial risk), the tax shield benefit more than compensates
✅ WACC falls from 18% to ~16.2% — the tax shield creates real value for shareholders.
Question B2 — MM No Tax: Cost of Equity After Gearing

📋 Scenario: Stellar plc

Stellar plc is currently all-equity financed with a firm value of £600 million and a cost of equity of 20%. The company plans to issue £200 million of permanent debt at 9% interest and use the proceeds to buy back shares. Assume MM with no taxes.

(a) What is the value of Stellar plc after the debt issue?
(b) What is the new cost of equity Ke?
(c) Verify that WACC remains unchanged.

📖 Click to reveal full worked solution

Part (a): Firm Value (MM No Tax)

V_L = V_U = £600m (capital structure irrelevant under no-tax MM)

The debt issue and share buyback simply rearrange the capital structure — total value is unchanged.

Equity value = V_L − D = £600m − £200m = £400m

Part (b): New Cost of Equity

D/E = £200m / £400m = 0.5
Ke = K0 + (K0 − Kd) × D/E
= 20% + (20% − 9%) × 0.5
= 20% + 11% × 0.5 = 20% + 5.5% = 25.5%

Part (c): Verify WACC Unchanged

We = 400/600 = 66.7% | Wd = 200/600 = 33.3%
WACC = 0.667 × 25.5% + 0.333 × 9%
= 17.0% + 3.0% = 20.0% ✓
✅ WACC = 20% = K₀ — unchanged, as MM no-tax predicts. The higher Ke exactly offsets the benefit of cheaper debt.

Key insight: Under MM no tax, gearing is a zero-sum game for shareholders. They get a higher return (25.5% vs 20%) but only because they're bearing more financial risk — not because any value has been created.
