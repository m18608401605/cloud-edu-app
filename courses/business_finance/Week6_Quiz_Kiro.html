Question 1 of 10
Pearson Ch.8 MCQ
Mozar plc, an all-equity financed business, currently has 20 million ordinary shares of £0.25 each. These are quoted at £1.70 each. The business is about to announce a three-for-ten rights issue at £1.20 each.

What will be the theoretical value of the right to buy one of the new shares?
A: £1.58
B: £0.50
C: £1.20
D: £0.38
✅ Answer: D — £0.38

Step 1: Calculate TERP (Theoretical Ex-Rights Price)
10 existing shares × £1.70 = £17.00
3 new shares × £1.20 = £3.60
Total value = £20.60 for 13 shares
TERP = £20.60 ÷ 13 = £1.585

Step 2: Value of Right
Value of right = TERP − Issue price = £1.585 − £1.20 = £0.385 ≈ £0.38

⚠️ Pitfall: Don't confuse TERP (£1.585) with the value of the right. The right is the benefit of buying at a discount, not the post-rights share price.
Question 2 of 10
Pearson Ch.8 MCQ
Which of the following sources of equity finance has the highest opportunity cost?
A: Rights issue
B: Public issue of new shares
C: Retained earnings
D: Bonus issue
✅ Answer: C — Retained earnings

Retained earnings represent profits that could have been paid as dividends to shareholders. Shareholders forgo that cash, so the opportunity cost equals the shareholders' required return — the same as ordinary equity.

⚠️ Pitfall: Many students think retained earnings are "free" because no cash leaves the firm. Wrong — the opportunity cost is real. Shareholders could have invested that money elsewhere at their required rate of return.
Question 3 of 10
Pearson Ch.8 MCQ
In a rights issue, what is the role of an underwriter?
A: To set the issue price above the market price
B: To guarantee that all shares will be sold by buying any unsold shares
C: To advise shareholders whether to take up their rights
D: To calculate the TERP for the issuing company
✅ Answer: B — Guarantee all shares will be sold

An underwriter agrees to purchase any shares not taken up by existing shareholders. This removes the risk of a failed issue for the company — in exchange for an underwriting fee.

⚠️ Pitfall: The issue price must always be below the current market price (not above) to make the rights attractive. If set above, no rational shareholder would exercise their rights.
Question 4 of 10
Pearson Ch.8 MCQ
When a company makes a rights issue, which of the following must be true?
A: The issue price must equal the current market price
B: All shareholders must take up their rights
C: The issue price must be above the current market price
D: The issue price must stay below the current market price
✅ Answer: D — Issue price must stay below market price

If the issue price equals or exceeds the market price, shareholders would simply buy shares in the open market instead. The discount is what makes rights valuable and ensures take-up.

Shareholders who don't want to exercise their rights can sell the rights in the market — so they are never forced to participate (B is false).
Question 5 of 10
Pearson Ch.8 MCQ
Which of the following methods of raising equity finance is generally the most expensive?
A: Rights issue
B: Placing
C: Bonus issue
D: Public issue of ordinary shares
✅ Answer: D — Public issue of ordinary shares

A full public issue (IPO or seasoned offering) involves extensive marketing, prospectus preparation, regulatory compliance, and underwriting fees — making it the most costly method.

Cost ranking (cheapest → most expensive):
Bonus issue (no cash raised) → Retained earnings → Rights issue → Placing → Public issue
Question 6 of 10
Pearson Ch.8 MCQ
Which of the following correctly ranks these securities from lowest to highest required return?
A: Ordinary shares < Preference shares < Government securities
B: Preference shares < Government securities < Ordinary shares
C: Government securities < Preference shares < Ordinary shares
D: Government securities < Ordinary shares < Preference shares
✅ Answer: C — Govt < Preference < Ordinary

Required return reflects risk. Government securities (gilts) are essentially risk-free. Preference shares have fixed dividends and priority over ordinary shareholders in liquidation — less risky than ordinary shares but riskier than gilts. Ordinary shareholders bear the most residual risk, so they demand the highest return.

Risk = Return: Higher risk → higher required return. This is the fundamental risk-return trade-off.
Question 7 of 10
Pearson Ch.8 MCQ
Which of the following is the most important source of equity finance for UK businesses?
A: Retained profit
B: Rights issues
C: Public issues of new shares
D: Venture capital
✅ Answer: A — Retained profit

In practice, retained earnings (ploughed-back profits) are by far the dominant source of equity finance for UK companies. Rights issues and public issues are used for large capital raises but are relatively infrequent.

Why? Retained earnings avoid flotation costs, don't dilute existing shareholders, and signal management confidence in future prospects.
Question 8 of 10
Pearson Ch.8 MCQ
How do you assess the following two statements?
(1) A warrant is a type of bond that gives the holder the right to buy shares at a fixed price.
(2) A finance lease is similar to a loan in that the lessee effectively owns the asset and bears the risks and rewards of ownership.
A: True, True
B: False, True
C: True, False
D: False, False
✅ Answer: B — False, True

Statement 1 — FALSE: A warrant is an option (not a bond) that gives the holder the right to buy shares at a fixed price within a specified period. It is typically attached to a bond as a sweetener but is itself an equity derivative.

Statement 2 — TRUE: Under a finance lease, the lessee uses the asset for most of its economic life, bears maintenance costs, and cannot cancel easily. It is economically equivalent to borrowing to buy — hence similar to a loan.
Question 9 of 10
Pearson Ch.8 MCQ
Approximately what are the long-run annual real returns for UK government securities and UK equities respectively?
A: ~1% and ~5.3%
B: ~3% and ~8%
C: ~5% and ~10%
D: ~0% and ~3%
✅ Answer: A — ~1% and ~5.3%

Historical UK data (Dimson, Marsh & Staunton) shows long-run real returns of approximately:
• Government bonds (gilts): ~1% per year
• Equities: ~5.3% per year

The equity risk premium (ERP) ≈ 5.3% − 1% = ~4.3% — the extra return investors demand for bearing equity risk over the risk-free rate.
Question 10 of 10
Pearson Ch.8 MCQ
Compared to secured debt, unsecured debt (loan stock) is best described as:
A: Expensive and safe
B: Cheap and safe
C: Cheap and risky
D: Expensive and risky
✅ Answer: C — Cheap and risky

From the company's perspective, unsecured debt is cheaper to issue (no asset pledging, less legal complexity). But from the lender's perspective, it is riskier — no collateral means lower recovery in default.

⚠️ Pitfall: "Cheap" here refers to issuance cost, not interest rate. Lenders actually charge higher interest on unsecured debt to compensate for the extra risk — so the coupon rate is higher, but flotation costs are lower.

Question B1 — Rights Issue Analysis

📋 Scenario: Mozar plc Rights Issue

Mozar plc currently has 20 million ordinary shares of £0.25 nominal value, quoted at £1.70 each. The company announces a 3-for-10 rights issue at £1.20 per share.

Shareholder A holds 1,000 shares and is deciding what to do with her rights.

(a) Calculate the Theoretical Ex-Rights Price (TERP).
(b) Calculate the value of one right.
(c) Show that Shareholder A's wealth is the same whether she:
   (i) exercises all her rights, or
   (ii) sells all her rights in the market.

📖 Click to reveal full worked solution

Part (a): TERP Calculation

10 shares × £1.70 = £17.00 (existing)
3 shares × £1.20 = £3.60 (new)
Total = £20.60 for 13 shares
TERP = £20.60 ÷ 13 = £1.585

Part (b): Value of One Right

Value of right = TERP − Issue price = £1.585 − £1.20 = £0.385

Alternatively: (Market price − Issue price) ÷ (n + 1) = (£1.70 − £1.20) ÷ (10/3 + 1) = £0.50 ÷ 13/3 = £0.385 ✓

Part (c): Shareholder A's Wealth Comparison

Shareholder A holds 1,000 shares → entitled to 300 rights (3 for every 10 held)

(i) Exercise all rights:

Pay: 300 × £1.20 = £360 cash outflow
New holding: 1,300 shares × £1.585 = £2,060.50
Net wealth = £2,060.50 − £360 = £1,700.50

(ii) Sell all rights:

Sell 300 rights × £0.385 = £115.50 cash inflow
Remaining: 1,000 shares × £1.585 = £1,585.00
Total wealth = £1,585.00 + £115.50 = £1,700.50
✅ Both strategies yield £1,700.50 — rights issues are wealth-neutral for shareholders who act rationally.

Key insight: The only way a shareholder loses wealth is if they do nothing — letting rights lapse means forfeiting the £115.50 value of the rights.

Question B2 — Debt Financing Costs

📋 Scenario: Nexus plc Debt Instruments

Part A — Straight Debt:
Nexus plc issues a bond with £100 nominal value, 7% coupon rate (paid annually). The company's corporation tax rate is 25%.

Part B — Convertible Bond:
Nexus also issues a convertible bond. Each £100 bond can be converted into 35 ordinary shares. The current market price of Nexus shares is £2.60.

(a) Calculate the after-tax cost of debt (as a % of nominal value).
(b) Calculate the conversion price of the convertible bond.
(c) Calculate the conversion premium (as a %).
(d) Explain why a company might prefer to issue convertible bonds rather than straight debt.

📖 Click to reveal full worked solution

Part (a): After-Tax Cost of Debt

Annual coupon = 7% × £100 = £7.00
Tax relief = £7.00 × 25% = £1.75
After-tax interest = £7.00 − £1.75 = £5.25
After-tax cost of debt = £5.25 ÷ £100 = 5.25%

Or simply: Kd(after-tax) = Coupon rate × (1 − tax rate) = 7% × (1 − 0.25) = 5.25%

Part (b): Conversion Price

Conversion price = Nominal value ÷ Conversion ratio
= £100 ÷ 35 shares = £2.857 per share

Part (c): Conversion Premium

Conversion premium = (Conversion price − Current share price) ÷ Current share price × 100
= (£2.857 − £2.60) ÷ £2.60 × 100
= £0.257 ÷ £2.60 × 100 = 9.9%

The bond converts at a 9.9% premium to the current share price — investors accept this because they get a fixed coupon in the meantime.

Part (d): Why Issue Convertibles?

Companies prefer convertibles over straight debt for several reasons:

  • Lower coupon rate: The conversion option is valuable, so investors accept a lower interest rate than on straight debt
  • Deferred equity dilution: Shares are only issued if/when conversion occurs — no immediate dilution
  • Attractive to growth companies: If the share price rises above the conversion price, bondholders convert — effectively raising equity at a premium to today's price
  • Tax efficiency: Interest payments are tax-deductible until conversion
✅ Convertibles are a "best of both worlds" instrument — cheaper than straight debt, less dilutive than immediate equity issuance.
