Week 2 - Module 2: The Double-Edged Sword of External Finance: Equity vs. Debt

Welcome to Module 2. When internal retained earnings are insufficient to fund a company's ambitious capital budgeting projects, the financial manager must turn to the external capital markets. The fundamental decision boils down to a critical trade-off: Should the firm sell ownership (Equity) or borrow money (Debt)? In this module, we will rigorously evaluate the mechanics, costs, and risks associated with the primary vehicles of external finance.

1. Equity Finance: Ordinary and Preference Shares

ğŸ“š Concept: Equity Instruments

Ordinary Shares (Common Stock): This forms the backbone of a firm's financial structure, representing the ultimate risk capital. Ordinary shareholders are the true owners. They bear the highest risk because they have a residual claimâ€”they receive dividends and liquidation proceeds only after all other obligations (debt and preference shares) are settled. In return, they possess voting rights that control the company's direction.

Preference Shares (Preferred Stock): A hybrid form of equity offering a lower risk profile. Preference shareholders receive a fixed rate of dividend which must be paid before any ordinary dividends. They rank ahead of ordinary shareholders in the event of liquidation but generally do not possess voting rights. They can be cumulative (unpaid dividends accumulate and must be cleared in future years) or non-cumulative.

Why would a company choose to issue equity instead of debt? The primary advantage of equity is the absence of mandatory cash outflows. If a company fails to pay a dividend, it does not trigger bankruptcy. However, equity is significantly more expensive than debt because investors demand higher returns to compensate for the residual risk.

Real-World Case Study: Tesla's Model 3 Liquidity Crunch
Between 2017 and 2018, Tesla was burning through massive amounts of cash to ramp up the production of its Model 3. Investors were highly concerned about a potential liquidity crisis. If Tesla had relied entirely on bank loans (debt), the mandatory interest payments would have bankrupted the firm before the cars were even manufactured. Instead, Tesla frequently tapped into the primary market to issue new equity. By selling ordinary shares, they successfully transferred the immense risk to investors who were willing to accept zero short-term dividends in exchange for the prospect of massive future capital gains. This perfectly illustrates the power of equity: it provides a shock absorber during high-risk, cash-burning growth phases.

Anti-Pitfall Strategy: A critical error students make is assuming that Preference Share dividends are tax-deductible, just like interest on debt. They are not. Dividends paid to preference shareholders come out of after-tax profits. This makes preference shares a highly unpopular form of long-term financing compared to debt.

Classic Exam MCQ:
Which of the following statements regarding ordinary and preference shares is correct?
A) Ordinary shareholders receive a fixed dividend regardless of profitability.
B) Preference shareholders generally possess the voting power to elect the board of directors.
C) In the event of liquidation, ordinary shareholders are paid before preference shareholders.
D) Ordinary shares represent residual ownership and carry unlimited potential returns but higher risk.

Answer & Rationale: D. A is false (dividends fluctuate). B is false (preference shares usually lack voting rights). C is false (preference ranks above ordinary). D precisely defines the nature of ordinary equity.

2. Debt Finance: Debentures, Bonds, and Covenants

ğŸ“š Concept: Debt Instruments and Safeguards

Loan Capital & Bonds: Unlike equity, debt represents a contractual obligation to pay regular interest and repay the principal amount at maturity. A Debenture is a long-term bond that is typically secured by a trust deed against the corporate assets.

Security (Charges): Lenders mitigate their risk by securing the debt against assets. A Fixed Charge is tied to a specific asset (e.g., a factory), preventing the firm from selling it. A Floating Charge "hovers" over a shifting class of assets (e.g., inventory) and only crystallizes into a fixed charge if the firm defaults.

Loan Covenants: Restrictive clauses written into the loan contract designed to protect the lenders. They limit the financial manager's freedom by setting maximum gearing ratios, restricting dividend payouts, or forbidding the issuance of further debt.

ğŸ‘¨â€ğŸ« Professor's Explanation & Application

Debt is generally the cheapest source of external finance for two reasons: (1) It carries lower risk for the investor due to legal guarantees and asset security, and (2) The "Tax Shield"â€”interest payments are legally allowable expenses that reduce the company's taxable profit. However, excessive debt introduces fatal financial distress risks.

Real-World Case Study: Bed Bath & Beyond's Debt Trap
For years, the retail giant Bed Bath & Beyond utilized massive amounts of cash to repurchase its own equity. While this initially looked brilliant, the capital structure became severely imbalanced. As the retail market shifted and sales plummeted, the firm was still contractually obligated to service its debt and adhere to stringent loan covenants. The lack of financial flexibility (unlike equity, debt interest cannot be "skipped" when times are tough) directly pushed the firm into Chapter 11 bankruptcy in 2023. Debt is a powerful accelerator, but it removes a firm's margin for error.

ğŸ“ Exam Anti-Pitfall Guide & Past Papers

Anti-Pitfall Strategy: In essay questions evaluating financing options, never state that a company should "always use debt because it is cheaper." You must demonstrate an understanding of the trade-off theory: the tax benefits of debt must be weighed against the increasing probability of bankruptcy costs and the restrictive nature of loan covenants.

Classic Essay Question: "Discuss the advantages and disadvantages for a company deciding to raise capital through the issue of debentures rather than ordinary shares." (15 Marks)

Distinction (A+) Answer Strategy: Structure your essay clearly. Advantages of Debt: Highlight the tax deductibility of interest (the tax shield), lower required rates of return compared to equity, and the fact that debt does not dilute the voting control of existing shareholders. Disadvantages: Focus heavily on the mandatory nature of cash outflows increasing bankruptcy risk (Gearing risk), and how strict Loan Covenants can paralyze management's operational flexibility during an economic downturn.

3. Hybrid Instruments: Convertibles and Warrants

ğŸ“š Concept: Blurring the Lines

Convertible Loans: A financial derivative that gives the investor the right, but not the obligation, to convert their loan into ordinary shares at a specified price and future date. Until conversion, they receive regular interest.

Warrants: Financial instruments usually attached to a bond issue as a "sweetener." They give the holder the right to purchase ordinary shares at a set price within a certain timeframe. Unlike convertibles, warrants can often be detached and traded separately from the underlying bond.

ğŸ‘¨â€ğŸ« Professor's Explanation & Application

Why would a CFO issue a convertible bond? Imagine you are managing a risky tech startup. Lenders are terrified to give you a straight loan unless you pay an extortionate 15% interest rate, which you cannot afford. By issuing a convertible loan, you offer the lenders a "hedge." You can negotiate a much lower interest rate (e.g., 5%), and in exchange, you grant them the right to become shareholders if your tech startup succeeds and the stock price explodes. Itâ€™s delayed equity financing that provides cheap debt today.

ğŸ“ Exam Anti-Pitfall Guide & Past Papers

Anti-Pitfall Strategy: Understand the timeline of cash flows. If an exam scenario asks how a warrant affects a firm's cash position when exercised: remember that when investors exercise a warrant, they must pay the pre-agreed strike price to the company. Thus, exercising warrants brings new cash inflows into the firm, whereas converting a convertible loan simply swaps existing debt for equity without generating new cash.
