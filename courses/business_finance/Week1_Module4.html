1. The Illusion of Profit Maximization

Definition: Profit maximization is the traditional economic view suggesting a firm should operate to maximize its short-term accounting earnings (Total Revenue minus Total Costs).

While this sounds intuitive, modern corporate finance rigorously rejects this as a primary corporate objective due to three fundamental problems:

  • Quantitative Difficulties (Manipulation): Accounting profit is highly subjective. It depends on accounting policies (e.g., depreciation methods, inventory valuation). Accounting profit does not equal cash flow.
  • Timescale (Short-termism): Profit maximization usually focuses on the current financial year. Managers may cut vital long-term investments (R&D, staff training) to artificially boost this year's profit, sacrificing the firm's long-term survival.
  • Ignores Risk: Profit figures make no allowance for the level of risk undertaken. A project with a 50% chance of generating Â£100,000 is objectively less desirable than a project with a 100% chance of generating the same amount, yet profit maximization treats them equally.

To understand why "profit" is a dangerous compass, you must realize that a company can report record-breaking accounting profits on its income statement while simultaneously going bankrupt because it has run out of cash. Profit is an opinion; cash is a fact.

Case Study: The Tesco Accounting Scandal (2014)

In 2014, Tesco PLC, the UK's largest retailer, faced intense competition and declining market share. To meet aggressive short-term profit targets (driven by the "Profit Maximization" mindset), executives pressured accountants to prematurely recognize commercial income from suppliers. They successfully, yet fraudulently, inflated their accounting profit by a staggering Â£263 million.

The Strategic Insight: When the manipulation was uncovered, Tesco's share price collapsed by over 50%, destroying billions in actual shareholder wealth. This case perfectly illustrates the "Quantitative Difficulties" flaw: because profit is an accounting construct, it incentivizes executives to manipulate the books rather than create genuine economic value.

2. The Modern Paradigm: Shareholder Wealth Maximization

ğŸ“š Concept: Wealth Maximization

ğŸ“š æ ¸å¿ƒæ¦‚å¿µï¼šè‚¡ä¸œè´¢å¯Œæœ€å¤§åŒ–

Definition: In corporate finance, the primary, overriding objective is to maximize the wealth of the company's owners (the shareholders). For a publicly-traded company, this is practically achieved by maximizing the current market price of ordinary shares.

Share price maximization is superior because the financial markets accurately reflect the true value of a firm based on three critical variables that profit ignores:

  • The Magnitude of expected cash flows: It tracks real cash, not accounting illusions.
  • The Timing of expected returns: It acknowledges the Time Value of Money (cash today is worth more than cash tomorrow).
  • The Risk associated with these returns: Investors discount highly uncertain future cash flows, heavily penalizing reckless managerial behavior.

To maximize wealth, a financial manager must evaluate every single corporate decision (Capital Budgeting, Capital Structure, Working Capital) through the lens of Net Present Value (NPV). If a project has a positive NPV, undertaking it will mathematically increase the share price, thereby increasing shareholder wealth.

Case Study: Amazon's Decades of "Zero Profit"

For its first two decades, Amazon reported virtually zero accounting profit, and sometimes massive losses. If Jeff Bezos had pursued "Profit Maximization," he would have stopped investing in R&D and warehouse infrastructure to make his current-year income statement look appealing. Instead, he pursued Wealth Maximization. He relentlessly reinvested every cent of cash flow into building Amazon Web Services (AWS) and global logistics. The financial markets recognized the massive long-term cash flow potential. Consequently, Amazon's share price skyrocketed, creating unprecedented wealth for shareholders, despite the total absence of short-term accounting profit.

Stakeholder Theory: Suggests a business should be viewed as a coalition of various interest groups (employees, suppliers, customers, community, government), not just shareholders.

Satisficing: Coined by Herbert Simon due to "Bounded Rationality," satisficing argues that instead of seeking to mathematically maximize returns to one group, managers should aim to provide each stakeholder group with a "satisfactory" or adequate outcome.

Is shareholder wealth the only thing that matters? In modern finance, we recognize a critical synthesis: You cannot maximize long-term shareholder wealth if you systematically abuse your stakeholders.

If a company pollutes the environment (ignoring the community) to save money, it will face massive government fines and consumer boycotts, which will ultimately destroy its share price. Therefore, excellent CFOs treat the needs of stakeholders (paying fair wages, implementing ESG policies) as essential constraints. They "satisfice" the stakeholders to ensure smooth operations, which serves as the very foundation required to achieve the primary goal: Maximizing long-term shareholder wealth.
