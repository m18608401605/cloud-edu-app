Section A — Multiple Choice Questions (Pearson Ch.10)
Question 1 of 10
Pearson Ch.10 MCQ
How do you assess the following two statements?
(1) Every change in the market price of loan notes affects their coupon rate.
(2) Every change in the market price of loan notes affects their yield.
A: False, False
B: False, True
C: True, False
D: True, True
✅ Answer: B — False, True

Statement 1 — FALSE: The coupon rate is fixed at issuance — it's a contractual obligation (e.g., 8% of £100 nominal = £8/year forever). Market price changes do not affect it.

Statement 2 — TRUE: Yield (YTM) = Annual coupon ÷ Market price. If the market price falls, the yield rises; if price rises, yield falls. Yield moves inversely with price.

⚠️ Pitfall: Don't confuse coupon rate (fixed, based on nominal value) with yield (variable, based on market price). This is one of the most common errors in debt valuation.
Question 2 of 10
Pearson Ch.10 MCQ
If a business pays a fixed proportion p of its after-tax profit as a dividend each year, has a reinvestment rate r, and a dividend growth rate g. The relationship between these factors is:
A: g = p(1 − r)
B: g = p/r
C: g = (1 − p)r
D: g = (1 − p)/r
✅ Answer: C — g = (1 − p)r

The dividend growth rate equals the retention ratio × return on investment:
• (1 − p) = proportion of profit retained (ploughed back)
• r = return earned on reinvested funds

Example: If p = 40% (pay out 40%), r = 15% → g = 0.6 × 15% = 9%

Intuition: Growth comes from reinvesting profits. The more you retain (1−p) and the better your returns (r), the faster dividends grow.
Question 3 of 10
Pearson Ch.10 MCQ
Cheng plc consistently pays 40% of its after-tax profit as an ordinary dividend. The return on investment is 18% a year and it is expected to remain at this level.

What is the growth rate of the business's dividends?
A: 10.8%
B: 7.2%
C: 60.0%
D: 18.0%
✅ Answer: A — 10.8%

Using g = (1 − p) × r:
• Payout ratio p = 40%, so retention ratio = 1 − 0.40 = 60%
• Return on investment r = 18%
• g = 0.60 × 18% = 10.8%

⚠️ Pitfall: Don't use p × r = 0.4 × 18% = 7.2% (option B). That's the payout ratio times return — wrong formula. Growth comes from what's retained, not what's paid out.
Question 4 of 10
Pearson Ch.10 MCQ
If P is the current share price, d is the dividend per share just paid, k is the cost of equity and g is the rate of growth, the Gordon dividend growth model can be stated as:
A: k = [d(1 + g)/P] + g
B: P = d/(k − g)
C: k = (g/P) + d(1 + g)
D: P = g/(k − d)
✅ Answer: A — k = [d(1 + g)/P] + g

The Gordon Growth Model rearranged for cost of equity:
Ke = D1/P0 + g

Since d is the dividend just paid (D0), the next dividend D1 = d(1+g). So:
k = d(1+g)/P + g ✓

Note on B: P = d/(k−g) uses D0 in the numerator — this is technically incorrect. The correct price formula is P = D1/(k−g) = d(1+g)/(k−g). Option B is a common textbook shorthand but not the precise form when d = D0.
Question 5 of 10
Pearson Ch.10 MCQ
An equity share in Alexander plc has just paid a dividend and its current market price is £3.75. The dividend that it has just paid was £0.288 per share; the investors' required return on the share is 12% pa.

The growth rate of the dividend is:
A: 4.00%
B: 12.00%
C: 8.00%
D: 7.35%
✅ Answer: A — 4.00%

Using Ke = D1/P0 + g, rearrange for g:
D1 = D0 × (1+g) = 0.288(1+g)
0.12 = 0.288(1+g)/3.75 + g
0.12 = 0.0768(1+g) + g
0.12 = 0.0768 + 0.0768g + g
0.0432 = 1.0768g
g = 0.0432/1.0768 = 4.01% ≈ 4%

⚠️ Pitfall: Don't use D0/P0 = 0.288/3.75 = 7.68% and then subtract from 12% to get 4.32% — that's an approximation. The exact method above gives 4%.
Question 6 of 10
Pearson Ch.10 MCQ
Anthony plc is partly financed by loan notes with a coupon rate of 10% a year, which were issued a few years ago for £100 for each £100 nominal. The current market price of the loan notes is £73 for each £100 nominal. The loan notes are perpetual (they will never be redeemed). The corporation tax rate is 30%.

What is the after-tax cost of the loan notes?
A: 10.00%
B: 13.70%
C: 9.59%
D: 7.00%
✅ Answer: C — 9.59%

For a perpetual bond, YTM = Annual coupon ÷ Market price:
Annual coupon = 10% × £100 = £10
YTM (pre-tax) = £10 ÷ £73 = 13.70%
After-tax cost = 13.70% × (1 − 0.30) = 13.70% × 0.70 = 9.59%

⚠️ Pitfall: Don't use the coupon rate (10%) as the cost — that was the yield at issuance when price = £100. Now the price is £73, so the yield has risen to 13.70%. Always use YTM based on current market price, then apply the tax shield.
Question 7 of 10
Pearson Ch.10 MCQ
Use of weighted average cost of capital (WACC) to determine the discount rate requires that certain assumptions be made.

Which of the following is NOT one of those required assumptions?
A: Use of the capital asset pricing model to deduce costs of capital is inappropriate.
B: There is a known target gearing ratio.
C: The costs of the elements of finance will not alter in the future.
D: The project is of similar risk to others undertaken by the business.
✅ Answer: A — CAPM is inappropriate (this is NOT a required assumption)

WACC can actually use CAPM to derive Ke — there's no assumption that CAPM is inappropriate. In fact, CAPM is one of the standard methods for estimating equity cost.

The actual required assumptions for WACC are:
• B: Known target gearing ratio (so weights are stable)
• C: Costs of capital won't change materially
• D: New project has similar risk to existing business

⚠️ Pitfall: This is a "NOT" question — read carefully. The question asks which is NOT an assumption, not which IS one.
Question 8 of 10
Pearson Ch.10 MCQ
Costas plc is financed by 50 million shares of £1 each, whose current market value is £2.50 per share, and £50 million 10% loan notes, currently quoted at £102 (per £100 nominal). Last year's interest has just been paid. The loan notes are due to be redeemed at par in exactly one year's time. The cost of equity has been estimated at 20% pa.

What is Costas plc's WACC? (Ignore taxation.)
A: 15.0%
B: 29.0%
C: 8.5%
D: 16.5%
✅ Answer: D — 16.5%

Step 1: Market values
Equity: 50m shares × £2.50 = £125m
Debt: £50m nominal × (102/100) = £51m
Total = £176m

Step 2: Cost of debt (YTM, 1-year bond)
Cash flows: Pay £102 today, receive £10 coupon + £100 redemption = £110 in 1 year
YTM = (110 − 102)/102 = 8/102 = 7.84%

Step 3: WACC
We = 125/176 = 71.0%, Wd = 51/176 = 29.0%
WACC = 0.710 × 20% + 0.290 × 7.84% = 14.2% + 2.27% = 16.5%

⚠️ Pitfall: Use market value of debt (£51m), not nominal (£50m). And calculate YTM from current market price — don't use the 10% coupon rate as the cost of debt.
Question 9 of 10
Pearson Ch.10 MCQ
In the year just ended, Apex plc made an after-tax profit of £50 million, out of which it has just paid a single dividend for the year of £20 million. Paying out this proportion of profit is expected to continue indefinitely. The business's cost of equity is 25% pa and its return on investments is 15% pa.

What is the market value of Apex plc's equity, to the nearest £1 million?
A: £112 million
B: £105 million
C: £136 million
D: £125 million
✅ Answer: C — £136 million

Step 1: Dividend growth rate
Payout ratio p = £20m/£50m = 40%
g = (1 − p) × r = (1 − 0.40) × 15% = 0.60 × 15% = 9%

Step 2: Next year's dividend D1
D1 = D0 × (1+g) = £20m × 1.09 = £21.8m

Step 3: Equity value (Gordon Growth Model)
V = D1 / (Ke − g) = £21.8m / (0.25 − 0.09) = £21.8m / 0.16 = £136.25m ≈ £136m

⚠️ Pitfall: Don't use D0 = £20m directly in the denominator. You need D1 = D0(1+g). Also, don't forget to calculate g first — it's not given directly.
Question 10 of 10
Pearson Ch.10 MCQ
How do you assess the following two statements?
(1) If a new investment project is to be financed out of a particular issue of equity or debt, rather than out of the business's retained profits, the cost of the particular source of finance should be used as the discount rate.
(2) Using CAPM to derive the discount rate provides a more theoretically sound approach than using WACC.
A: False, False
B: False, True
C: True, False
D: True, True
✅ Answer: B — False, True

Statement 1 — FALSE: The discount rate should reflect the risk of the project, not the source of financing. Using a specific funding source's cost ignores the overall capital structure and can lead to incorrect accept/reject decisions. The correct approach is to use WACC (or CAPM-adjusted rate for the project's risk).

Statement 2 — TRUE: CAPM is theoretically superior because it directly accounts for the project's systematic risk (β), rather than relying on the firm's existing capital structure. WACC assumes the new project has the same risk as the firm's existing assets — an assumption that often doesn't hold.

Question B1 — Equity Valuation (Gordon Growth Model)

📋 Scenario: Meridian plc

Meridian plc has just paid a dividend of £0.30 per share. The current share price is £4.20. Analysts expect dividends to grow at 5% per year indefinitely.

The company also has the following data: risk-free rate = 4%, market risk premium = 6%, beta (β) = 1.15.

(a) Calculate the cost of equity using the Dividend Growth Model.
(b) Calculate the cost of equity using CAPM.
(c) Comment on why the two estimates differ and which you would prefer to use.

📖 Click to reveal full worked solution

Part (a): Dividend Growth Model

D₁ = D₀ × (1+g) = £0.30 × 1.05 = £0.315
Ke = D₁/P₀ + g = £0.315/£4.20 + 0.05
Ke = 0.075 + 0.05 = 7.5%

Part (b): CAPM

Ke = Rf + β × (Rm − Rf) = 4% + 1.15 × 6%
Ke = 4% + 6.9% = 10.9%

Part (c): Comment on the Difference

The two methods give different results (7.5% vs 10.9%) because they measure different things:

  • DGM is backward-looking — it's based on current market price and expected dividends. It assumes the market has correctly priced the share and that dividends grow at a constant rate forever. Sensitive to the growth rate assumption.
  • CAPM is risk-based — it uses systematic risk (β) and market data. More theoretically rigorous but depends on accurate β estimation and a stable risk-free rate.

Preference: CAPM is generally preferred for its theoretical soundness. DGM is only reliable for mature, dividend-paying companies with stable growth. In practice, analysts often use both and take an average.

✅ Key insight: Neither method is perfect. Use both as a cross-check, and be transparent about assumptions.
Question B2 — Full WACC Calculation

📋 Scenario: Vantage plc

Vantage plc has the following capital structure:

• Ordinary shares: 40 million shares, current market price £3.50 each. Beta = 1.3, Rf = 4%, market risk premium = 5.5%.
• Loan notes: £60 million nominal, 8% coupon, currently trading at £95 per £100 nominal. The notes are perpetual.
• Corporation tax rate: 25%

(a) Calculate the market value weights for equity and debt.
(b) Calculate the cost of equity Ke using CAPM.
(c) Calculate the after-tax cost of debt Kd.
(d) Calculate Vantage plc's WACC.

📖 Click to reveal full worked solution

Part (a): Market Value Weights

Equity MV = 40m shares × £3.50 = £140m
Debt MV = £60m × (95/100) = £57m
Total = £140m + £57m = £197m
We = 140/197 = 71.1% | Wd = 57/197 = 28.9%

Part (b): Cost of Equity (CAPM)

Ke = Rf + β × (Rm − Rf) = 4% + 1.3 × 5.5%
Ke = 4% + 7.15% = 11.15%

Part (c): After-Tax Cost of Debt

For a perpetual bond: YTM = Annual coupon ÷ Market price

Annual coupon = 8% × £100 = £8 per £100 nominal
YTM (pre-tax) = £8 ÷ £95 = 8.42%
After-tax Kd = 8.42% × (1 − 0.25) = 8.42% × 0.75 = 6.32%

Part (d): WACC

WACC = We × Ke + Wd × Kd(after-tax)
WACC = 0.711 × 11.15% + 0.289 × 6.32%
WACC = 7.93% + 1.83% = 9.76%
✅ Vantage plc's WACC ≈ 9.76% — any new project must return more than this to create shareholder value.

Key reminders: (1) Use market value of debt (£57m), not nominal (£60m). (2) YTM uses market price, not coupon rate. (3) Apply tax shield to debt cost only — equity dividends are not tax-deductible.
