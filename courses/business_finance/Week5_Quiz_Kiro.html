Section A — Multiple Choice Questions (Pearson Ch.7)
Question 1 of 10
Pearson Ch.7 MCQ
How do you assess the following two statements?
(1) In the London Stock Exchange, it is quite common to find two securities that are perfectly positively correlated.
(2) In the London Stock Exchange, it is quite common to find two securities that are perfectly negatively correlated.
A: False, False
B: False, True
C: True, False
D: True, True
✅ Correct: C — True, False.

Statement 1 is TRUE: Perfect positive correlation (ρ = +1) is not common but does occur — e.g., two shares in the same industry with identical business models can move almost in lockstep.

Statement 2 is FALSE: Perfect negative correlation (ρ = −1) is extremely rare in practice. It would require one security to always go up exactly when another goes down — almost impossible in real markets. This is why complete risk elimination through diversification is theoretical, not practical.
Question 2 of 10
Pearson Ch.7 MCQ
How do you assess the following two statements?
(1) The standard deviation of returns of a portfolio is the weighted average of the standard deviations of the securities that make up the portfolio.
(2) The expected return of a portfolio is the weighted average of the expected returns of the securities that make up the portfolio.
A: False, False
B: False, True
C: True, False
D: True, True
✅ Correct: B — False, True.

Statement 1 is FALSE: This is one of the most important exam traps in portfolio theory. Portfolio standard deviation is NOT a simple weighted average — it depends on the covariance between assets. The formula is: σ²_p = w²_A σ²_A + w²_B σ²_B + 2·w_A·w_B·Cov(A,B). The covariance term means portfolio risk is usually less than the weighted average.

Statement 2 is TRUE: Expected portfolio return IS simply the weighted average: E(R_p) = w_A·E(R_A) + w_B·E(R_B). No covariance needed here.
Question 3 of 10
Pearson Ch.7 MCQ
Combining about 20 different, equally valued, holdings of equity shares listed on the London Stock Exchange will lead to the elimination of most of the portfolio's:
a) Total risk
b) Specific risk
c) Market risk
d) Systematic risk
✅ Correct: B — Specific risk.

Research shows that holding ~20 randomly selected shares eliminates most specific (unsystematic) risk — the company-specific risks like management failures, product recalls, or strikes. These risks are uncorrelated across firms, so they cancel out in a portfolio.

Why others are wrong:
• Total risk = systematic + specific. You can only eliminate the specific portion.
• Market risk / Systematic risk: These affect all companies simultaneously (recessions, interest rate changes) and cannot be diversified away — no matter how many shares you hold.
Question 4 of 10
Pearson Ch.7 MCQ
The standard deviation of returns of an investment is:
a) A measure of the range of possibilities
b) A measure of the dispersion of possible returns
c) A measure of the expected return
d) The square root of the expected value
✅ Correct: B — A measure of the dispersion of possible returns.

Standard deviation (σ) measures how spread out returns are around the expected value. A higher σ means returns are more variable — i.e., more risk. It is calculated as σ = √[Σ pᵢ(Rᵢ − E(R))²].

Why others are wrong:
• A: "Range" implies max minus min — that's a different, cruder measure.
• C: Standard deviation measures risk, not expected return (that's E(R) = Σ pᵢRᵢ).
• D: Standard deviation is the square root of variance, not the expected value.
Question 5 of 10
Pearson Ch.7 MCQ
The only circumstances where diversification is NOT beneficial in terms of risk reduction is where investments:
a) Are perfectly negatively correlated
b) Are negatively correlated but less than perfectly so
c) Are positively correlated but less than perfectly so
d) Are perfectly positively correlated (ρ = +1)
✅ Correct: D — Perfectly positively correlated (ρ = +1).

When ρ = +1, the two assets move in perfect lockstep. Combining them gives a portfolio standard deviation that is exactly the weighted average of the individual standard deviations — no risk reduction at all.

For any ρ < +1, diversification reduces portfolio risk below the weighted average. The lower the correlation, the greater the benefit. At ρ = −1, risk can theoretically be eliminated entirely.

Key rule: Diversification benefits exist whenever ρ < +1.
Question 6 of 10
Pearson Ch.7 MCQ
An asset with a negative beta will have an expected return:
a) Higher than the risk-free rate
b) Lower than the risk-free rate
c) Equal to the risk-free rate
d) Equal to the expected return on the market portfolio
✅ Correct: B — Lower than the risk-free rate.

Using CAPM: K_e = R_f + β(R_m − R_f)

If β < 0, then β(R_m − R_f) is negative (since R_m > R_f in normal markets). So K_e = R_f + (negative number) < R_f.

Why would anyone accept below-risk-free returns? A negative-beta asset acts as insurance — it tends to go UP when the market goes DOWN. Investors value this hedging property so much they accept lower expected returns. Gold is a classic example of a near-zero or negative beta asset.
Question 7 of 10
Pearson Ch.7 MCQ
Which one of the following statements about CAPM is correct?
a) CAPM can only be used to deduce the expected return of an individual Stock Exchange listed security
b) CAPM can only be used to deduce the expected return of an efficient portfolio of Stock Exchange listed securities
c) CAPM can only be used to deduce the expected return of individual assets, be they Stock Exchange listed or not
d) CAPM can be used to deduce the expected returns of any assets or portfolios of assets irrespective of whether they are held in efficient portfolios or not
✅ Correct: D — CAPM applies to any asset or portfolio, efficient or not.

This is a key strength of CAPM. Unlike mean-variance analysis (which only applies to efficient portfolios), CAPM gives a required return for any asset based solely on its beta — whether it's a listed share, an unlisted company, a real asset, or an inefficient portfolio.

This is why CAPM is so widely used in capital budgeting: you can estimate the required return for a specific project by finding a comparable listed company's beta.
Question 8 of 10
Pearson Ch.7 MCQ
In the context of modern portfolio theory, 'efficiency' means:
a) Rationally reflecting all information
b) Having no systematic risk
c) Having no specific risk
d) Having no risk
✅ Correct: C — Having no specific risk.

In portfolio theory, an "efficient" portfolio is one that is fully diversified — all specific (unsystematic) risk has been eliminated. Only systematic (market) risk remains, which cannot be diversified away.

Why others are wrong:
• A: "Rationally reflecting all information" is the definition of market efficiency (EMH) — a different concept entirely.
• B: No portfolio can eliminate systematic risk through diversification.
• D: Even a fully diversified portfolio still bears systematic risk — it can never have zero risk (unless it's the risk-free asset).
Question 9 of 10
Pearson Ch.7 MCQ
In 'two fund separation', what are the two funds?
a) The risk-free investment and any portfolio of risky investments which lies on the efficient frontier
b) The risk-free investment and the market portfolio
c) Shares and loan notes
d) The risk-free investment and shares
✅ Correct: B — The risk-free investment and the market portfolio.

Two-fund separation (Tobin's Separation Theorem) states that every investor's optimal portfolio is a combination of just two funds: the risk-free asset and the market portfolio (the tangency portfolio on the efficient frontier).

The investor's risk preference determines the mix (how much in each fund), but the risky portion is always the same market portfolio for everyone. This is the theoretical foundation for index investing.

Why A is wrong: It's not just any efficient portfolio — it must specifically be the market portfolio (the tangency point where the Capital Market Line touches the efficient frontier).
Question 10 of 10
Pearson Ch.7 MCQ
According to research evidence, CAPM is used by about what percentage of larger UK businesses in their assessment of investment projects?
a) More than 41%
b) 10 to 20%
c) 21 to 40%
d) Less than 10%
✅ Correct: C — 21 to 40%.

Survey evidence (cited in McLaney Ch.7) shows that around 21–40% of larger UK businesses use CAPM to estimate the cost of equity for investment appraisal. This is a significant minority — CAPM is theoretically dominant but not universally applied in practice.

Many firms use simpler approaches (e.g., a fixed hurdle rate) or the dividend growth model instead. CAPM's practical limitations (estimating beta, the equity risk premium) mean adoption is lower than its theoretical prominence suggests.

Section B — Calculation Question (Ch.7 Portfolio Theory)
Calculation Question
Two-Asset Portfolio Risk & CAPM Application

📋 Part (a) — Portfolio Variance

An investor holds two assets with the following characteristics:

ItemAsset AAsset B
Expected Return E(R)12%18%
Standard Deviation σ8%15%
Portfolio Weight60%40%

The correlation coefficient between A and B is ρ = 0.4.

Required: Calculate (i) the portfolio expected return, (ii) the portfolio variance and standard deviation.

📋 Part (b) — CAPM Required Return

The risk-free rate is R_f = 5% and the expected market return is R_m = 11%.

Asset X has a beta of β = 1.6. Asset Y has a beta of β = 0.7.

Required: Calculate the required return for each asset using CAPM. Which asset offers a higher required return, and why?

🔍 Click to reveal full solution

Part (a) — Portfolio Expected Return

\( E(R_p) = w_A \times E(R_A) + w_B \times E(R_B) \)
\( E(R_p) = 0.6 \times 12\% + 0.4 \times 18\% = 14.4\% \)

Part (a) — Covariance

\( Cov(A,B) = \rho \times \sigma_A \times \sigma_B = 0.4 \times 8\% \times 15\% = 48\%^2 \)

Part (a) — Portfolio Variance

\( \sigma^2_p = w^2_A \sigma^2_A + w^2_B \sigma^2_B + 2 w_A w_B \cdot Cov(A,B) \)
\( \sigma^2_p = (0.6)^2(64) + (0.4)^2(225) + 2(0.6)(0.4)(48) \)
\( = 0.36 \times 64 + 0.16 \times 225 + 0.48 \times 48 \)
\( = 23.04 + 36.00 + 23.04 = 82.08\%^2 \)
\( \sigma_p = \sqrt{82.08} \approx 9.06\% \)

Note: The weighted average of individual σ's = 0.6×8% + 0.4×15% = 4.8% + 6% = 10.8%. Portfolio σ (9.06%) is less than 10.8% — this is the diversification benefit from ρ = 0.4 < 1.

Part (b) — CAPM: Asset X (β = 1.6)

\( K_e = R_f + \beta(R_m - R_f) = 5\% + 1.6 \times (11\% - 5\%) \)
\( K_e = 5\% + 1.6 \times 6\% = 14.6\% \)

Part (b) — CAPM: Asset Y (β = 0.7)

\( K_e = 5\% + 0.7 \times 6\% = 9.2\% \)
Asset X requires a higher return (14.6% vs 9.2%) because it has higher systematic risk (β = 1.6 vs 0.7). Asset X amplifies market movements — when the market rises 1%, X rises 1.6%. Investors demand extra compensation for bearing this additional market risk.

📌 Exam Anti-Pitfall

The most common error: using σ_A and σ_B directly in CAPM instead of beta. CAPM uses beta (systematic risk only), not total standard deviation. A high-σ asset with low beta may have a lower required return than a low-σ asset with high beta.

Calculation Question 2
Expected Return, Variance & Covariance from Probability Scenarios

📋 Scenario Data — Two Shares: Apex plc & Beacon plc

An analyst assigns probabilities to three economic scenarios for the coming year:

ScenarioProbabilityApex ReturnBeacon Return
Boom0.2530%10%
Normal0.5012%12%
Recession0.25−8%14%

Required:

  1. Calculate E(R) and σ for both Apex and Beacon.
  2. Calculate Cov(Apex, Beacon) and the correlation coefficient ρ.
  3. An investor puts 50% in each. Calculate the portfolio E(R) and σ_p.
  4. Comment on the diversification benefit and what the covariance sign tells you.
🔍 Click to reveal full solution

Part 1 — Expected Returns

\( E(R_{Apex}) = 0.25(30\%) + 0.50(12\%) + 0.25(-8\%) = 7.5 + 6 - 2 = 11.5\% \)
\( E(R_{Beacon}) = 0.25(10\%) + 0.50(12\%) + 0.25(14\%) = 2.5 + 6 + 3.5 = 12\% \)

Part 1 — Variances & Standard Deviations

\( \sigma^2_{Apex} = 0.25(30-11.5)^2 + 0.50(12-11.5)^2 + 0.25(-8-11.5)^2 \)
\( = 0.25(342.25) + 0.50(0.25) + 0.25(380.25) = 85.5625 + 0.125 + 95.0625 = 180.75 \)
\( \sigma_{Apex} = \sqrt{180.75} \approx 13.44\% \)
\( \sigma^2_{Beacon} = 0.25(10-12)^2 + 0.50(12-12)^2 + 0.25(14-12)^2 = 0.25(4) + 0 + 0.25(4) = 2 \)
\( \sigma_{Beacon} = \sqrt{2} \approx 1.41\% \)

Part 2 — Covariance

\( Cov = 0.25(30-11.5)(10-12) + 0.50(12-11.5)(12-12) + 0.25(-8-11.5)(14-12) \)
\( = 0.25(18.5)(-2) + 0 + 0.25(-19.5)(2) = -9.25 + 0 - 9.75 = -19 \)
\( \rho = \frac{Cov}{\sigma_A \sigma_B} = \frac{-19}{13.44 \times 1.41} \approx \frac{-19}{18.95} \approx -1.00 \)

⚠️ ρ ≈ −1: Apex and Beacon are nearly perfectly negatively correlated — they move in opposite directions. This is the ideal diversification scenario.

Part 3 — Portfolio (50/50)

\( E(R_p) = 0.5(11.5\%) + 0.5(12\%) = 11.75\% \)
\( \sigma^2_p = (0.5)^2(180.75) + (0.5)^2(2) + 2(0.5)(0.5)(-19) \)
\( = 45.1875 + 0.5 - 9.5 = 36.1875 \)
\( \sigma_p = \sqrt{36.1875} \approx 6.02\% \)

Part 4 — Diversification Comment

The weighted average σ = 0.5×13.44% + 0.5×1.41% = 7.43%. The portfolio σ (6.02%) is lower — a saving of 1.41%. The negative covariance (−19) is the key: Apex performs best in booms, Beacon is stable across all scenarios. Combining them smooths out the boom/bust swings. The negative covariance term (−9.5) directly reduces portfolio variance, demonstrating why assets with negative or low correlation are valuable in a portfolio.
