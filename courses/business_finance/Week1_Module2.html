Week 1 - Module 2: Corporate Objectives & Agency Theory

Welcome to Module 2. After defining the fundamental decisions of financial management in Module 1, we must now establish the ultimate compass guiding those decisions. What is the true goal of a corporation? In this module, we will dispel the dangerous myth of "profit maximization," define the rigorous standard of "wealth maximization," and explore the inherent conflicts of interest known as the Agency Problem.

1. The Fallacy of Profit Maximization

ğŸ“š Concept: Profit Maximization

Definition: Profit maximization is the traditional economic view that a firm should operate in a manner that increases its short-term accounting earnings (revenue minus costs).

While intuitive to laypeople, modern finance completely rejects this as a primary corporate objective due to three fatal flaws:

  • Quantitative Difficulties (Manipulation): Accounting profit is highly subjective. It is easily manipulated by altering depreciation methods or inventory valuation. Profit does not equal actual cash flow.
  • Timescale (Short-termism): Maximizing profit focuses heavily on the current financial year. Managers might cut R&D or employee training to artificially boost this year's profit, sacrificing long-term survival.
  • Ignores Risk: Profit figures make no allowance for the level of risk undertaken. A CEO gambling company funds in a highly risky venture might generate a massive short-term profit, but stockholders detest such uncompensated risk.

To truly understand why "profit" is a deceptive metric, let us examine how easily executives can manipulate it to trigger their bonuses, simultaneously destroying the firm's true value.

Real-World Case Study: The Tesco Accounting Scandal (2014)
In 2014, Tesco PLC, the UK's largest retailer, was under immense pressure from discount chains. To meet aggressive short-term profit targets, Tesco executives actively encouraged the premature booking of commercial income from suppliers, effectively falsifying the timing of their revenues. They artificially inflated their accounting profit by an estimated Â£263 million.

The Result: When the "profit maximization" illusion collapsed, Tesco's share price plummeted by over 50%. Billions of pounds of shareholder wealth were wiped out, and senior management faced criminal charges from the Serious Fraud Office. This vividly proves that accounting profit is merely an opinion; cash flow is a fact.

Distinction (A+) Answer Strategy: A standard answer lists the definitions. An A+ answer demonstrates criticality. Use the PEEL structure (Point, Evidence, Explanation, Link). Argue that while profit targets are acceptable as secondary, short-term operational benchmarks, they are disastrous as strategic goals. Cite the Tesco Â£263m scandal as empirical evidence of the "manipulation" flaw, proving that profit-chasing can destroy real corporate value.

2. The True Goal: Shareholder Wealth Maximization

ğŸ“š Concept: Shareholder Wealth Maximization

Definition: The primary, overriding objective of corporate finance is to maximize the wealth of the company's owners (the shareholders). In publicly traded companies, this is practically achieved by maximizing the current market price of ordinary shares.

Share price is the ultimate surrogate for wealth because it naturally internalizes the three variables that profit ignores:

  • Magnitude of Cash Flows: It tracks real cash, not accounting illusions.
  • Timing of Cash Flows: It acknowledges the Time Value of Money (TVM).
  • Risk of Cash Flows: Investors discount expected future cash flows based on uncertainty.

ğŸ‘¨â€ğŸ« Professor's Explanation & Application

Unlike short-sighted profit, a company's stock price reflects the market's collective evaluation of all future cash flows the firm will ever generate. It forces managers to act sustainably.

Real-World Case Study: Amazon's Decades of Zero Profit
For its first two decades, Amazon reported virtually zero accounting profit. If Jeff Bezos were pursuing "profit maximization," he would have ceased investing in infrastructure to make his income statement look appealing. Instead, he pursued Wealth Maximization. He relentlessly reinvested cash into building AWS and global logistics. The financial markets recognized the massive long-term cash flow potential. Consequently, Amazon's share price skyrocketed, creating unprecedented wealth for shareholders, despite the absence of accounting profit.

ğŸ“ Exam Anti-Pitfall Guide & Past Papers

Anti-Pitfall Strategy: In Multiple Choice Questions (MCQs), examiners will try to trick you by mixing the terminology.

Classic Exam MCQ:
Which of the following best describes the fundamental objective of financial management?
A) To maximize the accounting profit of the firm.
B) To maximize the magnitude of short-term cash flows.
C) To maximize the current value per share of the existing stock.
D) To maximize the firm's market share against competitors.

Answer & Rationale: C. Maximizing current share value perfectly encapsulates the magnitude, timing, and risk of long-term wealth. Option A is the classic distractor. Option D is an operational goal, not the primary financial objective.

3. Agency Theory and Corporate Governance

ğŸ“š Concept: The Agency Problem

Definition: An agency relationship exists when principals (shareholders) hire agents (managers) to run the company on their behalf. The Agency Problem arises when conflicts of interest occurâ€”specifically, when managers prioritize their own welfare (e.g., job security, luxury perks, empire building) over the shareholders' goal of wealth maximization.

Corporate Governance refers to the system of rules, practices, and incentives used to align the interests of managers with those of the shareholders, thereby reducing "Agency Costs."

ğŸ‘¨â€ğŸ« Professor's Explanation & Application

If you own a company but hire someone else to run it, how do you stop them from buying a corporate jet with your money? Because of Information Asymmetry (managers know the daily data; shareholders only see the annual report), monitoring is difficult. The most effective solution is incentive alignment.

Real-World Case Study: Disney's Executive Compensation
The Walt Disney Company has millions of dispersed shareholders, making the agency problem highly potent. To prevent CEO Bob Iger from coasting, the Board of Directors utilized an aggressive incentive mechanism. Iger's base salary was set at a relatively modest $1 million, but he was offered stock awards and performance-based bonuses valued at over $25 million. By making his personal wealth entirely dependent on Disney's stock performance, they mathematically aligned the agent's interests with the principals'.

Distinction (A+) Tip: When discussing how to solve the agency problem using stock options, you must show Critical Thinking. An A+ student will point out the flaw in stock options: During a macro-economic boom (a bull market), stock prices rise universally. Therefore, a CEO might see their stock options become worth millions purely due to market momentum, not because of their actual managerial skill. This is a critical limitation of incentive schemes.
