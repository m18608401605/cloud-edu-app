Question 1 of 10
Pearson Ch.6 MCQ
Which of the following is the most serious problem with sensitivity analysis?
a) It requires a computer to carry out
b) It is too complex for most managers to understand
c) It is difficult to estimate input values
d) It only works for short-term projects
✅ Correct: C — It is difficult to estimate input values.

Sensitivity analysis asks "what if one variable changes?" — but the whole exercise is only useful if you can reliably estimate the range of that variable. In practice, forecasting future sales volumes, costs, or discount rates is notoriously hard.

Why others are wrong:
• A: Sensitivity analysis can be done on a simple spreadsheet — no special software needed.
• B: It's actually one of the simpler risk tools — managers find it intuitive.
• D: It applies equally to long-term capital investment decisions.
Question 2 of 10
Pearson Ch.6 MCQ
Which method involves simultaneously changing a number of variables to represent a particular set of circumstances?
a) Sensitivity analysis
b) Scenario building
c) Simulation
d) Expected value analysis
✅ Correct: B — Scenario building.

Scenario building constructs coherent "stories" about the future (e.g., "recession scenario" or "boom scenario") where multiple variables change together in a consistent way. This is its key distinction.

Why others are wrong:
• A: Sensitivity analysis changes one variable at a time, holding others constant.
• C: Simulation (Monte Carlo) randomly samples from probability distributions — it's more sophisticated and computer-intensive.
• D: Expected value uses probabilities to calculate a weighted average outcome — it doesn't "build scenarios."
Question 3 of 10
Pearson Ch.6 MCQ
A project has 2 possible selling prices, 3 possible variable costs, 4 possible fixed costs, and 5 possible sales volumes. How many possible outcomes does this project have?
a) 14
b) 60
c) 120
d) 240
✅ Correct: C — 120.

When variables are independent, total combinations = product of all possibilities:
2 × 3 × 4 × 5 = 120

This illustrates why simulation is needed for complex projects — manually evaluating 120 scenarios is impractical, let alone thousands.

Why others are wrong:
• A (14): This is the sum (2+3+4+5), not the product — a common mistake.
• B (60): Missing one factor — perhaps only multiplying 3×4×5=60.
• D (240): Overcounting — perhaps doubling the correct answer.
Question 4 of 10
Pearson Ch.6 MCQ
Which of the following best describes specific (unsystematic) risk?
a) Risk that affects all investments equally
b) Risk that relates to a particular investment but not to most other investments
c) Risk that cannot be reduced through diversification
d) Risk measured by the market beta coefficient
✅ Correct: B — Risk that relates to a particular investment but not to most other investments.

Specific (unsystematic) risk is company-specific — e.g., a factory fire, a CEO scandal, a product recall. It affects one firm but not the broader market.

Key insight: Because specific risk is uncorrelated across firms, it can be diversified away by holding a portfolio. Rational investors won't be compensated for bearing it.

Why others are wrong:
• A: That describes systematic (market) risk — e.g., interest rate changes, recessions.
• C: This is the definition of systematic risk — it cannot be diversified away.
• D: Beta measures systematic risk (sensitivity to market movements).
Question 5 of 10
Pearson Ch.6 MCQ
What does it mean to say an investor is indifferent between two points on the same indifference (utility) curve?
a) The investor prefers the point with higher return
b) The investor prefers the point with lower risk
c) The investor is irrational
d) The individual does not mind where they are on that curve
✅ Correct: D — The individual does not mind where they are on that curve.

An indifference curve represents combinations of risk and return that give the investor equal utility. Moving along the curve (accepting more risk for more return, or vice versa) leaves the investor equally satisfied — they are truly indifferent.

Why others are wrong:
• A & B: If the investor preferred one point over another, they would not be on the same indifference curve — they'd be on different curves.
• C: Indifference is a rational concept in utility theory — it means equal satisfaction, not irrationality.
Question 6 of 10
Pearson Ch.6 MCQ
A risk-averse investor facing an uncertain investment would:
a) Pay exactly the expected value of the investment
b) Pay more than the expected value
c) Not pay as much as the expected value
d) Be indifferent between the investment and its expected value
✅ Correct: C — Would not pay as much as the expected value.

A risk-averse investor dislikes uncertainty. Given a choice between a certain £100 and a gamble with expected value £100, they prefer the certain £100. Therefore, they would only buy the risky investment at a discount to its expected value — the discount represents their "risk premium."

Why others are wrong:
• A: Paying exactly the expected value describes a risk-neutral investor.
• B: Paying more than expected value describes a risk-seeking investor (e.g., a gambler who enjoys the thrill).
• D: Indifference between risky and certain outcomes of equal expected value = risk-neutral.
Question 7 of 10
Pearson Ch.6 MCQ — True/False
Statement 1: Objective probabilities are based on the opinions of experts.
Statement 2: Diversification involves investing in a range of projects or securities so that the returns are not perfectly positively correlated.

Which combination is correct?
a) True, True
b) False, True
c) True, False
d) False, False
✅ Correct: B — False, True.

Statement 1 is FALSE: Objective probabilities are based on historical data and observed frequencies (e.g., "this machine fails 3% of the time based on 10 years of records"). Probabilities based on expert opinion are called subjective probabilities.

Statement 2 is TRUE: Diversification works precisely because different assets don't move in perfect lockstep. If returns are not perfectly positively correlated (correlation < +1), combining them reduces overall portfolio risk.
Question 8 of 10
Pearson Ch.6 MCQ — True/False
Statement 1: Most businesses do not formally consider risk when making investment decisions.
Statement 2: Sensitivity analysis is the most widely used method of risk assessment in practice.

Which combination is correct?
a) True, True
b) False, True
c) True, False
d) False, False
✅ Correct: B — False, True.

Statement 1 is FALSE: Survey evidence consistently shows that the majority of businesses formally consider risk in capital investment decisions — through sensitivity analysis, scenario analysis, or adjusted discount rates.

Statement 2 is TRUE: Sensitivity analysis ("what if" analysis) is the most commonly used risk assessment tool in practice because it is simple, intuitive, and requires no probability estimates. McLaney cites survey evidence confirming this.
Question 9 of 10
Pearson Ch.6 MCQ — Calculation
A project has two possible outcomes:
• 60% probability: NPV = £10 million
• 40% probability: NPV = −£3 million

What is the value of perfect information about which outcome will occur?
a) £1.8 million
b) £3.0 million
c) £2.4 million
d) £6.0 million
✅ Correct: C — £2.4 million.

Step 1: Expected NPV without perfect information
E(NPV) = 0.6 × £10m + 0.4 × (−£3m) = £6m − £1.2m = £3.6m
(Since E(NPV) > 0, the firm would proceed with the project.)

Step 2: Expected NPV with perfect information
With perfect info, the firm only invests when the good outcome occurs:
E(NPV | perfect info) = 0.6 × £10m + 0.4 × £0 = £6m
(When bad outcome is predicted, the firm walks away — NPV = £0, not −£3m.)

Step 3: Value of perfect information
EVPI = £6m − £3.6m = £2.4 million
Question 10 of 10
Pearson Ch.6 MCQ
When assessing a high-risk project, a firm should (compared to a normal project):
a) Increase the discount rate and reduce the maximum payback period
b) Decrease the discount rate and increase the maximum payback period
c) Increase the discount rate only
d) Reduce the maximum payback period only
✅ Correct: A — Increase the discount rate and reduce the maximum payback period.

Two common practical adjustments for high-risk projects:

1. Higher discount rate: Adds a risk premium to the cost of capital, making the NPV hurdle harder to clear. Riskier cash flows are worth less in present value terms.

2. Shorter payback period: Requires the investment to be recovered faster, reducing exposure to long-term uncertainty. The further into the future, the less reliable the forecasts.

Both adjustments work in the same direction — they make it harder for risky projects to be approved, which is appropriate.

📌 Exam Anti-Pitfall

Common mistake: forgetting to subtract fixed costs before multiplying by the annuity factor. Fixed costs are annual cash outflows — they must be included in the annual net cash flow, not treated separately.
